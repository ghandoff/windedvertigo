teacher's guide · anchor scenario

lakeshore college sustainability committee

bring institutional operations to net-zero greenhouse-gas emissions by 2050, without compromising educational quality, equity of access, or institutional solvency.

learning outcome

students distinguish parameter, rules, and paradigm-level interventions in an institutional system, and explain why higher tiers bend long-run trajectories that lower tiers cannot.

key concept

the dominant intervention here is the paradigm-level re-charter. it wins because every emission category at lakeshore — mobile, stationary, embodied — is downstream of the question the institution is asking itself. parameter changes (parking, heat pump) sag and partially recover because they leave the underlying mandate untouched: "operate the campus." rules changes (building code, voting) bend the curve more, but they remain instrumental — they serve the existing purpose. re-chartering changes the purpose, so every later decision is now being evaluated against a dual mandate. the multiplier on every other intervention is the lever.

the trade-off this scenario teaches

the secondary stock is political capital — the committee's standing with its members, the trustees, and the campus at large. every intervention costs some authority before it pays back, and most never fully recover. the parking permit burns political capital fastest and buys the least emissions per unit spent. the heat pump drops capital for the whole ramp and can be abandoned mid-plan when a new chair arrives. the rules-level moves (building code, binding votes) spend early capital, reach equilibrium, and hold. the re-charter is the only intervention that pays back more authority than it spends — but only after a deep early fall that, for eighteen months, looks like committee suicide. watching both charts is what makes the difference between "higher tier wins" and "higher tier rewards patience."

common misconceptions to surface

  • "the biggest capital project must be the highest leverage." capital scale is not the same as structural depth — the heat pump is expensive but parameter-level.
  • "the re-charter alone solves it." it doesn't — it multiplies the others. without lower-tier work to multiply, the paradigm shift produces rhetoric, not tons.
  • "governance is soft, infrastructure is real." in this system the governance change (binding votes) outperforms two infrastructure interventions on its own.

three debrief questions

  1. which intervention felt most realistic to propose at your institution, and what does that reveal about where you have authority — and where you don't?
  2. if your institution were to re-charter, what would the new purpose actually be? who would resist, and on what grounds?
  3. where in your school does a "parking permit" stand in for a structural change that no one wants to name?

lesson plans

15 minutes — solo play + 5 min debrief

  1. 10 min: students play the scenario individually, committing to one intervention with a written hypothesis.
  2. 5 min: facilitator names the leverage tiers; students share their surprise answers.

30 minutes — solo play + pair discuss + debrief

  1. 10 min solo play, including hypothesis commit.
  2. 10 min pair discussion: compare hypotheses, surprise answers, and whether the political-capital curve was visible to each pair when they chose.
  3. 10 min facilitator-led debrief on the three debrief questions — and on which intervention pays back more authority than it costs.

60 minutes — context-set + solo + pair + group + transfer

  1. 10 min context-set: meadows' twelve leverage points, condensed to three tiers.
  2. 15 min solo play.
  3. 10 min pair discussion.
  4. 15 min full-group debrief on the three questions.
  5. 10 min transfer prompt: name one parameter, one rules, one paradigm intervention live in your own institution right now.
