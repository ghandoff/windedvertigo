saas churn

reverse the customer attrition trend and build a base that grows itself through retention and referral.

learning outcome

students evaluate meadows' caution that "the leverage point is not always where you expect it," and distinguish a system whose binding constraint sits at the parameter layer from one whose bottleneck is structural.

key concept

this is the load-bearing scenario in the library: paradigm does not win here, because the binding constraint is at the parameter layer. the dominant intervention is the discount on annual renewals — a parameter change. the product works; customers leave because the perceived value does not justify the price at the current activation rate. adjusting the price-to-value ratio releases the bottleneck directly. repositioning (the paradigm move) is valuable but slower and less certain — it changes who arrives, which only eventually addresses the activation problem upstream. the lesson: tier height is a diagnostic vocabulary, not a ranking. when the bottleneck is at the parameter layer, a parameter fix holds.

the trade-off this scenario teaches

the secondary stock is revenue per customer. the discount — intervention 1 — is the dominant move on the primary and also the most expensive move on the secondary: customers stay, margin per customer drops by the size of the discount. counting heads can disguise a bleeding ledger. notice, though, that intervention 4 (onboarding redesign) is both structurally correct and protects the secondary — higher activation supports willingness to pay. so the deeper lesson compounds: paradigm does not win here, and the move that does win is also the one that doesn't betray the second ledger. two structural virtues in the same intervention.

common misconceptions to surface

  • "discounts always decay." in most systems they do — in this one, price is the binding variable, so the fix holds.
  • "repositioning is structurally superior." the paradigm shift takes quarters to propagate; meanwhile the constraint stays gripped.
  • "higher tier = better lever." the ladder ranks structural depth, not effectiveness on a given stock. always look for the binding constraint first.

three debrief questions

  1. what evidence would convince you that a problem you treat as cultural is actually a parameter problem in disguise?
  2. when has a "structural" intervention in your work failed because it bypassed the actual binding constraint?
  3. how would you test, before committing, whether a system's bottleneck sits at the parameter, rules, or paradigm layer?

lesson plans

15 minutes — solo play + 5 min debrief

  1. 10 min: solo play, commit with hypothesis.
  2. 5 min: name the tiers; surface the surprise that the parameter wins.

30 minutes — solo play + pair discuss + debrief

  1. 10 min solo play.
  2. 10 min pair discussion: where each pair's intuition went, and why.
  3. 10 min group debrief on binding constraints — and the margin chart. who at this company would notice the margin damage? who wouldn't?

60 minutes — context-set + solo + pair + group + transfer

  1. 10 min context-set: meadows' "the leverage point is not always where you expect it"; binding constraints in operations research.
  2. 15 min solo play.
  3. 10 min pair discussion.
  4. 15 min group debrief.
  5. 10 min transfer prompt: name a problem in your work that you have been treating one tier higher than it deserves.
