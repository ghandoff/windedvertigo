reimagining PEDAL Conference 2025

an experience design proposal for the annual conference of the PEDAL Centre, University of Cambridge — rethinking the format so the day says the same thing the research does.

PEDAL (Play, Education, Development And Learning) is a research centre at the University of Cambridge. what follows shows how the format of their annual conference could be redesigned to embody the same principles the research advocates.

2 weeks before

before the day

every attendee receives a question to sit with, a note about the day's design, and a name badge with a QR code linking to a personal conference page.

this is not a standard conference. there are moments that ask something of you. all of them are optional. none of them are difficult.

a companion app linked to badge QR codes threads the digital layer through the whole experience — before, during, and after the day.
9:00 a.m.

the question wall

as people arrive, they add to a wall that already holds children's responses. by 09:30 it holds sixty to seventy cards. it stays up all day.

9:40 a.m.

Stephen Morgan MP

the minister's team receives three children's quotes from the question wall the week before. his keynote opens with those quotes. thirty seconds of silence. then he responds to each one as if the child were in the room.

after the talk, attendees write one sentence on a card: "the policy I'd make if I were six." the minister reads five of them aloud. the room hears its own imagination alongside the policy.
10:10 a.m.

the making space

cardboard, tape, children's prompt cards, coloured blocks. the brief:

using only what is here, make something a child could learn from.

pick up the cardboard. read the children's cards. or just watch. all of these are the activity.

drag the pieces around
10:40 a.m.

hall of mirrors & bumper cars

two formats running together. people from outside the play research field sit at tables with a question from the morning — ideas refracted through a different lens. short, timed conversations between people who would not normally sit together.

how do children learn what risk means?

architect
children test structural limits every time they stack. they already understand load-bearing.
games developer
play without failure states is not play. the best games let you fail safely a hundred times.
chef
a child cooking is a child doing chemistry with their hands. mess is methodology.
product designer
children prototype constantly. they just don't call it that.

11:45 a.m.

choose your room

both sessions run simultaneously. both rooms are the right room.

session a

building a community for policy change

collaborative workshop on translating research into policy language and forming advocacy networks.

session b

neurodiversity-affirming play

exploring how play environments can be designed to affirm and include neurodivergent children.

12:25 p.m.

the collage

a collage station runs through lunch. add something from this morning that is still with you — a phrase, a sketch, a question. it grows through the break. it will be displayed at the close.

beetles need houses too · mess is methodology · watch this · the gap between what we study and how we gather
1:30 p.m.

risky play

thirty seconds of unedited footage. children in risky play. sound on. no framing. no commentary.

Prof Ellen Beate Hansen Sandseter

the audience had just seen what she was about to analyse, from the children's perspective, before the adult interpretation arrived.

after the keynote, the Boulind: a short opt-in activity with genuine uncertainty. you might build something that could fall, write a response you cannot take back, or try a physical challenge calibrated to your own threshold.
2:15 p.m.

three tracks running simultaneously

main hall

AI panel

instead of talking about AI and play, the audience would experience it. each table receives a toy and a prompt generated live by a language model. the panel watches the room play first, then responds to what they saw.

the boulind

the quiet room

low light. no task. no facilitation. a place to sit with what the day had produced so far. needing quiet is not opting out. it is a different way of being in.

click to dim the lights
networking space

connecting play networks

each table opened with one question, drawn from children's responses to being asked what they wish adults understood about play.

3:15 p.m.

map your context

a short paired exercise. what assets already exist where you work? who is already doing something? what have you built that has not been named yet?

the template is there for people who think better with a structure in front of them. the conversation option is there for people who think better out loud.

you leave with a map of your own context — named assets, named people, named gaps. ten minutes. no debrief required.
