political capital
the move that bends the primary the most is rarely the move that protects the secondary. notice which interventions cost what — and which, eventually, pay back more than they cost.
the higher you push on the leverage ladder, the more the system bends. the bottom of the ladder produces a sag and a recovery. the top of the ladder produces a structural break.
what this system is like
a campus is a slow-growth system with long feedback delays. emissions rise not because of one bad decision, but because the default structure rewards expansion and defers consequences.
how to spot the pattern
parameter tweaks (pricing, efficiency) produce a dip, then a recovery — the system absorbs them. structural changes (governance, purpose) bend the curve permanently because they change which questions get asked.
which lever matters most
the highest-leverage intervention is not the most expensive one. it is the one that changes the rules under which every other decision is made. meadows called this "the power to transcend paradigms."
note: this model is calibrated to illustrate leverage-point dynamics, not to forecast actual emissions. the relative ordering of interventions — not the absolute numbers — is the lesson.
