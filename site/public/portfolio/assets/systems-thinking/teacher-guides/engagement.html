teacher's guide · academic

student engagement

move weekly active participation from compliance-level to a sustained classroom culture where students engage with what they're learning, not what they're being graded on.

learning outcome

students evaluate why compliance-level interventions produce a spike-and-return pattern, and distinguish rules-level from paradigm-level moves that change what "engagement" means.

key concept

the dominant intervention is the paradigm-level reframing — from "content delivery" to "building capacity to act." it wins because every compliance lever (points, reminders, shorter lectures) leaves the underlying purpose intact: students are still performing participation rather than participating in something they care about. the paradigm shift redefines what counts as engagement and multiplies every other lever. without it, the ratchet of compliance reasserts itself within weeks.

common misconceptions to surface

  • "more incentives produce more engagement." they produce more performance — until students find the floor.
  • "better course design solves engagement." design helps, but only inside the existing purpose. a redesigned course delivering the wrong thing performs the same shape.
  • "paradigm shifts are ungradable." they are gradable — what changes is what you grade.

three debrief questions

  1. what does your course actually optimise for, and how would you know if it changed?
  2. which of your students' behaviours are compliance, and which are commitment? how can you tell them apart?
  3. if "engagement" were redefined in your syllabus tomorrow, what would you stop doing?

lesson plans

15 minutes — solo play + 5 min debrief

  1. 10 min: solo play, commit with hypothesis.
  2. 5 min: name the tiers; share surprises.

30 minutes — solo play + pair discuss + debrief

  1. 10 min solo play.
  2. 10 min pair discussion: where compliance ends and commitment begins.
  3. 10 min group debrief.

60 minutes — context-set + solo + pair + group + transfer

  1. 10 min context-set: balancing loops in motivation; why incentives plateau.
  2. 15 min solo play.
  3. 10 min pair discussion.
  4. 15 min group debrief.
  5. 10 min transfer prompt: rewrite one course objective so it names the capacity, not the topic.
