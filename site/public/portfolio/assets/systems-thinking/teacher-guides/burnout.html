team burnout

restore sustainable team energy in a planning system whose every "good sprint" raises the bar on the next one.

learning outcome

students analyse a ratchet pattern in resource systems, and distinguish parameter interventions that subsidise coping from rules-level interventions that interrupt the structural mechanism producing the problem.

key concept

the dominant intervention is the rules-level scope cap. it wins because the planning process uses last sprint's output as next sprint's floor — a structural ratchet that parameter interventions (stipends, no-meeting days, contractors) cannot interrupt. capping scope at 80% of capacity puts the captured party (the team) at the planning table structurally: the cap exists because someone measured their capacity and made it binding. the paradigm reframing (from "shipping features" to "sustainable delivery of value") helps but cannot outrun the planning mechanism. without the structural cap, the ratchet reasserts.

the trade-off this scenario teaches

the secondary stock is sprint velocity. the rules-level winner — scope cap — appears for a year to slow the team down, because it is measured against a velocity metric that was never sustainable. year two shows what the old velocity was actually costing in defects, attrition, and planning accuracy. the paradigm reframing drops raw story-point velocity too, because the denominator changes: "value shipped" replaces "features shipped." watching both charts is what lets a team tell the story of the cap without the narrative collapsing into "we slowed down."

common misconceptions to surface

  • "more capacity solves overload." it doesn't — work expands to fill the new capacity unless the planning rule changes.
  • "culture change is the deepest fix." here, redefining success without changing the planning mechanism produces statements, not slack.
  • "wellness benefits address burnout." they subsidise recovery from the system without changing the system.

three debrief questions

  1. where in your work does last quarter's output silently become next quarter's minimum?
  2. who is at the planning table in your organisation, and who is the "captured party" — the people the plan is happening to?
  3. what would change if the team's capacity, rather than its appetite, were the binding input?

lesson plans

15 minutes — solo play + 5 min debrief

  1. 10 min: solo play, commit with hypothesis.
  2. 5 min: name the tiers; share surprises.

30 minutes — solo play + pair discuss + debrief

  1. 10 min solo play.
  2. 10 min pair discussion: where the ratchet shows up in each pair's work, and what the velocity chart would do during the first six months of a cap.
  3. 10 min group debrief on the three questions — how would you defend year one to a leadership team that only reads the velocity number?

60 minutes — context-set + solo + pair + group + transfer

  1. 10 min context-set: ratchet patterns and the "captured party" in planning systems.
  2. 15 min solo play.
  3. 10 min pair discussion.
  4. 15 min group debrief.
  5. 10 min transfer prompt: name one cap your organisation could honestly defend.
