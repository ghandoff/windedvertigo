teacher's guide · sustainability / institutional

office plastic

reduce weekly single-use plastic in an office whose every "campaign" produces a visible dip and a return — the signature of substitution rebound.

learning outcome

students analyse substitution rebound in disposal systems, and distinguish awareness/access changes from rules-level closure of the procurement channel.

key concept

the dominant intervention is the rules-level procurement ban — closing the buying channel, not the disposal channel. it wins because every parameter intervention above (recycling bins, signage, free reusables) tried to change behaviour while the default remained available. once disposable cannot be procured, substitution rebound becomes structurally impossible — there is nothing to substitute back to. the paradigm shift (from "reduce waste" to "zero disposable culture") extends and deepens the change but does not outperform the procurement rule on this specific stock; the structural bottleneck was at the rules layer, not the goal layer.

the trade-off this scenario teaches

the secondary stock is staff time spent administering the programme. every parameter intervention adds a programme: recycling means collection and sorting; signage means maintenance; reusable mugs means distribution, replacement, and coordination. the rules-level intervention (procurement ban) is the only move that removes ongoing admin — one hard negotiation, then the programme discharges itself. the paradigm reframing expands scope across departments before consolidating, so its admin chart rises before it settles. watching both charts makes visible why "more initiatives" is so often the enemy of "less work, less plastic."

common misconceptions to surface

  • "recycling and signage reduce plastic." they manage the disposal channel; the procurement channel keeps refilling the building.
  • "giving people alternatives is enough." it is not — alternatives compete with a default that remains available every day someone forgets their mug.
  • "the goal statement is the lever." here the goal change helps, but the structural move was closing the buying channel.

three debrief questions

  1. where in your operations does an "alternative" coexist with the default it was meant to replace?
  2. which awareness campaigns in your work have produced the dip-and-return pattern?
  3. what would your procurement team need from leadership to make a default unavailable?

lesson plans

15 minutes — solo play + 5 min debrief

  1. 10 min: solo play, commit with hypothesis.
  2. 5 min: name the tiers; surface the substitution-rebound shape.

30 minutes — solo play + pair discuss + debrief

  1. 10 min solo play.
  2. 10 min pair discussion: name a substitution-rebound pattern from each pair's workplace, and the admin cost of whichever programme runs it.
  3. 10 min group debrief on the three questions — who in your operations would notice the staff-hours drop when the procurement ban discharges the programme?

60 minutes — context-set + solo + pair + group + transfer

  1. 10 min context-set: substitution rebound; default channels in choice architecture.
  2. 15 min solo play.
  3. 10 min pair discussion.
  4. 15 min group debrief.
  5. 10 min transfer prompt: identify one default in your operations whose removal would change behaviour faster than any campaign.
