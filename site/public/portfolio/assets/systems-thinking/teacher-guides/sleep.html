teacher's guide · personal

sleep debt

restore average nightly sleep to a sustainable level before the compounding effects of sleep debt become chronic.

learning outcome

students analyse a reinforcing decline loop and distinguish environmental nudges from rules-level boundaries and paradigm-level redefinitions of productivity.

key concept

the dominant intervention is the paradigm-level redefinition of productivity — from "hours worked" to "quality of decisions made when rested." it wins because the pressure that creates sleep debt is the belief that more hours awake equal more output. environment changes (curtains, melatonin) help at the margins; the bedtime rule helps because it creates a defendable boundary; but the redefinition removes the underlying incentive to stay up. without it, the rule erodes to "just one more thing."

the trade-off this scenario teaches

the secondary stock is self-reported daytime productivity. the rules-level fix — a hard bedtime — produces a six-month dip on the productivity chart as evening output collapses before rest quality rises. watching that recovery live is the pedagogical move; most people give up on the bedtime rule somewhere inside the dip. the paradigm shift dissolves the trade-off by re-anchoring the metric: "focused hours" becomes "quality of decisions made when rested," and the chart shape changes because the chart is now counting a different thing. two charts are what make the redefinition legible rather than a rhetorical dodge.

common misconceptions to surface

  • "better information about bedtime fixes the behaviour." reminders work for a week, then fade.
  • "a better environment is enough." quality matters once you're in bed; the problem is what keeps you out.
  • "productivity and rest are in tension." in a redefined frame, they are the same thing measured differently.

three debrief questions

  1. what does your work culture reward — hours visible or decisions made?
  2. where does the "I'll sleep when I'm dead" identity show up in your team, and what does it cost?
  3. what rule could you make tonight that would still hold in three months?

lesson plans

15 minutes — solo play + 5 min debrief

  1. 10 min: solo play, commit with hypothesis.
  2. 5 min: name the tiers; share surprises.

30 minutes — solo play + pair discuss + debrief

  1. 10 min solo play.
  2. 10 min pair discussion: which interventions each pair has actually tried, and whether they abandoned any inside the productivity dip.
  3. 10 min group debrief on the three questions — what would your team's productivity number do under a bedtime rule, and whose evaluation of your work would change?

60 minutes — context-set + solo + pair + group + transfer

  1. 10 min context-set: reinforcing decline loops; debt that compounds silently.
  2. 15 min solo play.
  3. 10 min pair discussion.
  4. 15 min group debrief.
  5. 10 min transfer prompt: rewrite one definition of "productive" in your own working life.
