supply chain transparency

raise the share of verified tier-2 suppliers in a system whose defining feature is an 18-month feedback delay between sourcing decisions and visible consequences.

learning outcome

students analyse a delay-dominant system and distinguish structural-feedback compression from rules-level enforcement.

key concept

the dominant intervention is publishing the supplier list — technically a parameter change (visibility, not rules), but in a delay-dominant system it is the highest-leverage move because compressing the feedback loop is the structural change. the 18-month gap between sourcing and consequences is the feature that allowed opacity to persist; publication collapses it. the rules-level requirement (tier-2 disclosure as a condition of preferred-supplier status) helps but propagates through the same delay; the paradigm-level redefinition takes years to reach tier-2 vendors. in delay-dominant systems, the leverage point is the delay itself.

the trade-off this scenario teaches

the secondary stock is procurement cost. transparency isn't free: the move that compresses the feedback delay (publishing the supplier list) also produces a brief exposed-supplier premium, and the rules-level intervention (required disclosure) commands a sustained premium from compliant suppliers. the paradigm redefinition is most expensive upfront, as a new compliant-supplier market forms. watching both charts is what prevents the conversation collapsing into "transparency costs too much" — the cost is real, and it is less than the downstream cost of a violation surfacing eighteen months late on national television.

common misconceptions to surface

  • "forms with more questions produce better data." they signal interest; suppliers answer what's easy.
  • "audit budgets buy verification." they buy throughput, not structural pressure.
  • "transparency is soft compared with a contract clause." in delay-dominant systems, transparency is the structural move; the contract clause remains downstream of the same lag.

three debrief questions

  1. where in your organisation does an 18-month feedback delay quietly determine what gets noticed and what gets buried?
  2. which of your reporting practices compress feedback, and which simply add it later?
  3. what would your procurement team do differently if every contract were public the day it was signed?

lesson plans

15 minutes — solo play + 5 min debrief

  1. 10 min: solo play, commit with hypothesis.
  2. 5 min: name the tiers; surface that the parameter wins because it compresses delay.

30 minutes — solo play + pair discuss + debrief

  1. 10 min solo play.
  2. 10 min pair discussion: locate a delay in each pair's work, propose a compression move, and estimate the short-term cost premium it would carry.
  3. 10 min group debrief on the three questions — who inside your organisation holds the procurement cost budget, and how would you brief them on the cost curve?

60 minutes — context-set + solo + pair + group + transfer

  1. 10 min context-set: meadows on delays as the leverage point in delay-dominant systems.
  2. 15 min solo play.
  3. 10 min pair discussion.
  4. 15 min group debrief.
  5. 10 min transfer prompt: identify one delay in your supply chain or operations whose compression would change behaviour upstream.
