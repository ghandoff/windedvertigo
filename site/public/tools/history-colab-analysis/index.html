01 — who they are

history co:lab at a glance

history co:lab is a civic innovation lab that puts humanity at the center of learning. founded through a 2024 merger of the original history co:lab and ithrive games, it operates today under co-leadership of fernande raine and susan rivers.

their mission: create the conditions for young people to grow into their power, grounded in history, connected to community, and equipped for civic life. they work across four service lines — thought leadership, product design, strategic advising, and convening + weaving — with active community of practice programmes in kansas city, pittsburgh, and dallas.

notable outputs include untextbooked — an award-winning youth-led podcast with 90,000+ downloads across 100 countries and an ambie award nomination — a co-designed us history curriculum funded by xq, and a growing library of research publications co-authored with partners including the carnegie foundation and rockefeller brothers fund.

02 — critical assessment

where the site is working against itself

the site is honest, readable, and intellectually credible — which reflects the organisation well. the mission language is strong and the content has real weight. but the site is working against itself in several structural and experiential ways. issues fall into four areas.

technical & hygiene

these are the most fixable problems and should be addressed regardless of whether a full redesign happens.

multiple legacy pages still publicly accessible and google-indexed

the following urls are all live and indexed by search engines:

  • /aboutus — labelled 'about - older'
  • /about-us — labelled 'old - our team'
  • /about-us-3 — a newer iteration
  • /about-2025 — appears to be the current intended about page
  • /our-programs — labelled 'old - our projects'

this creates a fragmented seo footprint and means visitors arriving from google may land on outdated, contradictory pages. for an organisation that just completed a significant merger and rebrand, this is actively undermining first impressions. old pages should be redirected or unpublished.

donate button links off-site to a different domain

the donate button — which appears in primary navigation — routes to got-history.org/donate, a completely separate domain. the same is true for the 'co:ntribute, co:create, co:llaborate' cta links on the resources page. this is a legacy artifact from the organisation's previous brand identity.

from a user's perspective: i clicked donate and i'm suddenly at a different website? this erodes trust and almost certainly costs donations. the got-history.org domain should be evaluated for consolidation, redirect, or replacement.

browser page title shows 'general 2' on the resources page

the resources page (/resources-2025) displays 'general 2 — history co:lab' in the browser tab. this is a squarespace internal page name that was never updated. it reads as unfinished and appears in browser history and bookmarks.

shopping cart icon visible in the header

a '0 items in cart' link appears in the site header — a default squarespace element for e-commerce templates that was never disabled. for a mission-driven nonprofit, a shopping cart icon is disorienting. it implies a store that doesn't exist.

navigation & information architecture

the site currently offers five navigation items: home, resources, about, engage, donate. for an organisation with the programmatic complexity of the co:lab, this is too compressed. key programmes and entry points are invisible from the top level.

programmes have no home in the navigation

untextbooked, the creation co:lab, and the learning ecosystem work in kansas city, pittsburgh, and dallas are all active, named programmes — but none appear in the main navigation. a visitor curious about the organisation's actual work has to hunt.

the engage page is thin to the point of confusion

the engage page contains a join form and a donate button — that's it. the homepage, by contrast, elegantly segments audiences: young people, educators, and partners. the engage page abandons that structure.

the homepage does the information architecture work that the engage page should be doing. the visitor who clicks 'engage' has already self-identified — the page should meet them there.

about dropdown has only one sub-item

the about dropdown currently reveals only 'our team.' if the intent is a dropdown, there should be richer sub-navigation — or the dropdown should be removed in favour of a single, well-structured about page.

content & storytelling

the content quality is genuinely strong in places. the merger narrative is compelling, the four-service framework is clear, and the publications on the resources page are legitimate thought leadership. but some key stories are missing or misplaced.

the merger story deserves more real estate

the 2024 merger of history co:lab and ithrive games is a significant organisational moment — two pioneering organisations combining forces. currently, this story is buried near the bottom of both the homepage and the about page. for a first-time visitor trying to understand 'who are these people and why should i trust them,' this is probably the most compelling thing on the site.

no blog, news, or announcements section

the resources page lists publications with real timestamps (april 2025, october 2025) — which signals an active organisation. but there's no feed, no blog, no way for a returning visitor to see what's new. for an organisation doing original research and thought leadership, the absence of a content publishing stream is a missed opportunity for both engagement and seo.

impact numbers are present but could be sharper

the homepage references 'ways we are changing the game' with a carousel of impact items. carousels are one of the least effective ways to communicate impact — most users don't engage with them. untextbooked's 90,000 downloads across 100 countries is a genuinely impressive stat that deserves a more prominent, static treatment.

the youth voice is present but underdeveloped visually

for an organisation whose entire proposition is centred on young people's agency and co-design, the site should feel like young people had a hand in building it. currently youth partners are given a small listing under the team section.

visual presence

based on a review of the live site, the visual design is clean and functional but not distinctive. it reads as a respectable squarespace nonprofit template that hasn't been pushed far enough to feel ownable.

logo lockup is not optimised for screen

the 'history' text runs vertically on the left side of the co:lab mark. this is an interesting design choice but at smaller screen sizes the vertical text becomes nearly unreadable. the logo may also be creating legibility issues in the header where it competes with nav links.

the site doesn't yet reflect the post-merger identity

the co:lab describes itself as 'innovators and weavers' and its logo features a tree with roots symbolising growth and connectivity. but the current site visual language doesn't carry that metaphor through. there's no consistent illustration style, no visual thread connecting sections, and the page layouts feel like independent rooms rather than a coherent environment.

the footer undersells the organisation

the footer contains: an address, an email, a linkedin link, and a copyright notice. there's no final call to action, no newsletter pitch, no quick links to key pages, and no indication of the organisation's broader social presence. for many visitors, the footer is the last thing they see before deciding whether to engage.

03 — alignment with winded.vertigo

where we converge and where we complement

the strategic and philosophical overlap between the co:lab and winded.vertigo is substantial. these are not two organisations whose interests intersect at the margins — they share a core conviction about how young people learn, what learning is for, and what it looks like when it works.

where we converge

  • learning by making and doing — both believe in experiential, participatory learning as the foundation, not the supplement
  • co-design with young people — co:lab's ithrive games model and w.v's game design work share a commitment to designing with, not for
  • the humanities as a lever — history, storytelling, and creative expression as pathways to civic identity
  • ecosystem thinking — both operate as weavers and connectors, not just direct service providers
  • play and games — ithrive games and w.v's game portfolio create immediate common ground around the pedagogical case for play
  • small, mission-led teams with a big-tent collaborative model

where we complement

  • winded.vertigo brings deep creative design practice — the co:lab could benefit from w.v's approach to prototyping, visual identity, and digital experience design
  • the co:lab brings institutional reach — school partnerships, museum relationships, research publications, and funder networks that w.v doesn't yet have
  • w.v has international experience (idb/el salvador, unicef, sesame workshop); the co:lab is us-focused but globally-cited
  • the whirlpool / monthly community learning format is a natural analog to the co:lab's convening + weaving service line

what a collaboration could look like

modelwhat it could mean in practice
skills exchange winded.vertigo redesigns the co:lab's website; co:lab provides introductions to schools, museums, or funders in the civic learning space
paid engagement fernande has fundraising capacity — a grant or funder-backed web redesign project is a legitimate revenue opportunity for w.v
co-designed programme a joint learning experience format combining w.v's game design methodology with the co:lab's civic education content — could live as a whirlpool edition or co:lab workshop
research partnership w.v's creative platforms (creaseworks, gotcha!) + co:lab's research infrastructure could produce publishable work on creativity and civic learning

closing note

what this could be

history co:lab is an organisation with a genuinely strong story that its website is not yet fully telling. the content quality, mission clarity, and programmatic depth are all there — the site just needs a structure and visual language equal to the work.

for winded.vertigo, this is the kind of project that plays directly to our strengths: learning design meets web design; co-creation methodology meets brand development; a relationship-first engagement with a collaborator whose values align with ours.

whether this is a skills exchange, a funded project, or the beginning of a longer collaboration, the fit is real. the most important first step is a good conversation — and these questions are designed to make that conversation go somewhere.
