# PEDAL Concept Brief

## Jamie Boulding, winded.vertigo

### The Opportunity

The PEDAL Conference brings together researchers, policymakers, practitioners, and educators who care about play, education, and child development. The speakers are strong. The content matters. The format — panels, keynotes, breakout sessions — does what conference formats always do: it presents knowledge sequentially and hopes the room absorbs it.

The opportunity is to redesign the architecture around the content so the format says the same thing the research does. Every session at PEDAL argues that children learn through doing, through agency, through multiple modes of engagement. The day should work the same way.

### Design Principles

1. **The content stays intact.** No speaker loses their slot. No session is cut. What changes is what happens between and around the presentations.
2. **Multiple ways in.** Every activity has more than one entry point. Watching is participation. Writing is participation. Sitting quietly is participation.
3. **The day accumulates.** Each moment builds on the last. By the close, the room has produced something together that did not exist at 08:30.
4. **Children's voices run through the day.** Not as decoration. As counterpoint. The people the conference is most about are not in the room. Their words are.

### Pre-Materials

Two weeks before the conference, every attendee receives:

- A name badge with a QR code linking to a personal conference page
- A single question: "What do children understand about play that adults have forgotten?"
- A note about the day's design: this is not a standard conference. There are moments that ask something of you. All of them are optional. None of them are difficult.

The companion app links to the badge QR. It gives access to the question wall, the curiosity display updates, and — after the day — a curated summary of what the room produced.

### The Reimagined Day

**08:30 — Arrival**

People arrive and find the question wall already started. Cards from children are pinned alongside responses from attendees who answered the pre-conference question. The wall builds in real time as people add their own.

**09:00 — Registration & Question Wall**

The question wall builds in real time. People write on cards provided or add via the companion app. Children's responses are already there — distinguished by a warm border and italic text. The question sits at the centre: "What do children understand about play that adults have forgotten?" By 09:30, the wall holds sixty to seventy responses. It stays up all day.

**09:40 — Stephen Morgan MP**

Runs as planned. The minister walked through the same question wall as everyone else on the way in.

**10:10 — Dr Bo Stjerne Thomsen / LEGO Foundation — Making Space**

After the keynote, the Boulind opens as a making space. Tables hold cardboard, tape, children's prompt cards, coloured blocks, paper tubes. The brief is simple: "Using only what is here, make something a child could learn from." Pick up the cardboard. Read the children's cards. Or just watch. All of these are the activity.

**10:40 — Hall of Mirrors & Bumper Cars**

Two formats running together over refreshments. Hall of Mirrors: people from outside the play research field — an architect, a games developer, a chef, a product designer — sit at tables with a question from the morning. Ideas refracted through a different lens. Bumper Cars: a deliberate collision of perspectives. Short, timed conversations between people who would not normally sit together. The format produces friction. That is the point.

**11:10 — Dr Catherine Draper**

What the data shows. Alongside what children said. The children's quotes appear not as illustration but as counterpoint — a second voice running through the presentation that the audience holds alongside the research findings.

**12:25 — Lunch & Collage**

A collage station runs through lunch. The brief: "Add something from this morning that is still with you." A phrase, a sketch, a question, a photograph from the making space. The collage grows through the break. It will be displayed at the close.

**13:30 — Prof Ellen Beate Hansen Sandseter — Risky Play**

The session opens with thirty seconds of unedited footage. Children in risky play. Sound on. No framing. No commentary. The audience sees what Sandseter is about to analyse — from the children's perspective — before the adult interpretation arrives.

After the keynote, the Boulind offers a short opt-in activity with genuine uncertainty in it. Something where the outcome is not known before you try. Available in physical and non-physical forms. The brief is the same for everyone. The entry point is not.

**14:15 — Three Simultaneous Tracks**

The AI panel runs in the main hall as planned. A panel discussion on AI, toys, and the future of play.

The quiet room opens in the Boulind. Low light, no task, no facilitation. A place to sit with what the day has produced so far. Some people need noise and conversation to process. Some need stillness. Both are legitimate. Both are available. Needing quiet is not opting out. It is a different way of being in.

Connecting play networks — the networking session. Opens with one question on each table, drawn from children's responses to being asked what they wish adults understood about play.

**15:15 — Dr Jermaine D'Sa & Dr Andrea Grenier + Mapping**

After the session, a short paired exercise: map your own context. What assets already exist where you work? Who is already doing something? What have you built that has not been named yet? Available in writing, in conversation, or via a printed template. The template is there for people who think better with a structure in front of them. The conversation option is there for people who think better out loud. Ten minutes. No debrief required.

The output: participants leave with a map of their own context — named assets, named people, named gaps.

**16:15 — Prof Jenny Gibson — Closing**

The collage is displayed at the front. The room produced these today. The conference ends by acknowledging that the people it is most about were not in the room, and asking what that means for next year. The children's voices are named in the close.

On every chair: a card. "What is one thing you want to try, change, or pay attention to in the next two weeks, however small?" Take it with you or leave it on the table. The garden party invitation is on the back.

**16:30 — Garden Party**

Opt-in. The garden. If you want to stay in touch with people from today, the badge QR will have a simple directory in one week.

### After the Day

The badge QR page becomes the post-conference resource. A curated summary of what the room produced: key questions from the question wall, images from the making and collage exercises, the curiosity display as it looked at close of day. A simple directory of who was there and what they are working on. The children's quotes that ran through the day, included at the bottom. A prompt: "Return to something from the day."
