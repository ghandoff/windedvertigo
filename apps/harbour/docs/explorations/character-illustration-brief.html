illustration brief · v1 · april 2026

harbour material cast

A brief for Aaron Fruit — everything you need to know about our seven characters, their four poses each, the palette, the motion, and how they want to live across the harbour. What we've built so far is a working sketch; what we're asking you to do is make them sing.

The cast, at a glance

Seven characters, each the "host" of one way matter behaves. They're not mascots and they're not illustrations of specific objects — they're the family heads of form×function. Every material a kid might bring into a playdate (button, bottle, blanket, marshmallow, paint) belongs to one of these seven families. The characters ARE the taxonomy, but nobody says so out loud; kids discover it through repetition.

Thumbnails above are the current placeholder sketches (inline SVG, kid register). They work at tile size (40–48px) but feel handmade-but-unfinished. Aaron's brief: preserve the silhouettes and palettes, elevate the craft to Sago Mini / Oliver Jeffers range. Each character also has a full interactive exploration file below with all four poses.

The three-word vibe

When in doubt, lean toward these three words:

crayon drawer, warm

Think about the specific shade of cream in a child's scribble journal. Think about the way Jon Klassen's hats sit lopsided on his rabbits. Think about the surface of a favourite wooden toy after three years of being slept with. We're not after polish — we're after treasured. The characters should feel like objects a kid would pick up off a rug and put back down gently.

Reference aesthetic

Lean into

  • ABC Duolingo — simplicity that reads at tiny sizes.
  • Sago Mini — warm textures, character-first, big eyes with soft weight.
  • Oliver Jeffers — expressive line, emotive stillness, dignified goofiness.
  • Jon Klassen — flatness that carries story, restrained palette.
  • Richard Scarry's cleanest work — multi-character scenes with consistent language.

Avoid

  • Comic Sans infantilism (nothing about the cast condescends).
  • Rainbow gradients, neon, skeuomorphic shadows.
  • Calarts / Disney-style mouth-wide-open expressions.
  • Generic flat-illustration Dribbble "corporate Memphis."
  • Clipart-style outlines at uniform stroke weight.
  • Anything with a brand mascot vibe — these are a cast, not spokespeople.

Shared design language

Silhouette tells

Each character has one signature detail that makes their silhouette recognisable at thumbnail size without the body. A kid should be able to say "that's a Drip" from the gloss highlight alone. This is the load-bearing craft decision of each character — preserve and elevate. (Per-character tells are listed in each section below.)

Eyes + catchlights

Every character (except Mud) uses the same eye primitive: ink circle + cream catchlight offset up-left by 1.2px. That offset is what gives them a present look rather than a blank-stare-at-the-camera look. Mud is the exception — sleepy eyes (half-closed crescents) because malleable matter is the stillest of the seven.

Motion vocabulary

Characters breathe. Each has an idle animation matching its material behaviour:

  • Cord — slow sway, 5s cycle, ±3° rotation from pivot.
  • Twig — impatient tap, 4s cycle, brief tip-quiver.
  • Swatch — corner ripple, 6s cycle, gentle corner lift.
  • Jugs — slow blink, 8s cycle, eyes close for 80ms.
  • Crate — settle, 7s cycle, 1px vertical nudge.
  • Mud — slow breath, 11s cycle, subtle scale (±0.02).
  • Drip — undulation, 6s cycle, surface tension wobble.

We reserve the right to revise these once your illustrations land — the motion should emerge from the form you draw. Consider them a starting point.

Two registers — kid and adult

Each character has two variants:

  • Kid register — bright saturated colours, textured ink-wobble strokes, cream catchlights in the eyes. Used in creaseworks, paper.trail, feel-cards — anything kid-facing.
  • Adult register — muted sand palette, flat rendering, no wobble filter. Used in vertigo-vault, depth-chart, rubric-co-builder — anything adult-facing (research, assessment, professional-development surfaces).

The adult register is NOT just "desaturated kid register." It's a quieter version of the same character — same silhouette, same identity, different energy. Think: a children's-book character on the cover (kid) versus the author's working sketch (adult).

Cord

flexible · linear · the bendy rhyme
sand #c99460 sand-dk #7d6a45 blush #e89b8a ink #241c1e

Identity

Cord is a short rope at rest, a curl when tired, a knot when solving, a bridge when asked. Hosts: pipe cleaner, rubber band, yarn, ribbon, twine, wire, zip tie, string — anything kids can bend. The silhouette tell is the frayed end: a tiny threadbare tip that says "this was once longer."

Four poses (names in production)

  • base — the curl at rest, eyes forward.
  • knot — tied in a loose overhand, expression pleased.
  • coil — wound flat, eyes closed in concentration.
  • bridge — stretched taut between two points, eyes alert.

What to preserve

  • Frayed end — non-negotiable silhouette tell.
  • Ochre / sand colourway — Cord is earthy, not synthetic.
  • Slow sway motion vocabulary.

Invitations

  • Texture of the cord — right now it's a smooth solid shape. Could it have a faint braid, a twisted fibre, a painted grain?
  • The frayed end could become signature character-specific (individual hairs, a specific frayed direction per pose).
  • Expression range: Cord is the goofiest of the seven — lean into mischief on knot and bridge.

Twig

rigid · linear · the stiff rhyme
sand-dk #7d6a45 bark #5a4a2f blush #e89b8a ink #241c1e

Identity

Twig is Cord's stiff rival — pointing, direct, a bit impatient. Hosts: pencil, crayon, popsicle stick, toothpick, nail, straw, chalk, marker. The silhouette tell is the knot bump: one small protrusion along the shaft that marks where a branch used to grow. It breaks the line just enough to read as organic.

Four poses

  • base — horizontal stick, eyes midway.
  • point — held up at angle, eyes looking up.
  • cross — two Twigs crossed (maybe the single character pose shows an X of two limbs).
  • snap — broken mid-shaft, eyes surprised.

What to preserve

  • Knot bump — silhouette tell.
  • Bark texture (not smooth like Cord).
  • Impatient micro-motion.

Invitations

  • Is Twig one stick or could it host pencils too? The character should feel natural in either context (forest twig OR coloured pencil).
  • Consider a painted-tip variant for mark-maker contexts (chalk, crayon).

Swatch

flat · soft · the drape rhyme
champagne #ffebd2 sand #d9bf8a blush #e89b8a ink #241c1e

Identity

Swatch drapes, folds, layers, wraps. Hosts: paper, foil, fabric scrap, felt, construction paper, cardstock, bandana, napkin. The silhouette tell is the folded corner — one upper corner turned back to show a slightly darker underside, visible in every pose.

Four poses

  • base — flat square at rest with folded corner.
  • cover — draped over an invisible lump.
  • wrap — folded around something, visible seam.
  • cushion — bunched up into a puff.

What to preserve

  • Folded corner — silhouette tell.
  • Champagne / cream fill (Swatch is the lightest of the seven).
  • Ink strokes should show the paper weight — not too clean.

Invitations

  • Can Swatch have a faint texture suggesting either paper or fabric depending on context? We don't need two variants, just a texture that reads as "soft sheet."
  • Swatch's expression when cushion-ed is hard to place — invite your interpretation.

Jugs

rigid · hollow · the vessel rhyme
white #ffffff redwood cap #b15043 blush #e89b8a ink #241c1e

Identity

Jugs holds things. Hosts: plastic bottle, bottle cap, jar, water bottle, trash bin, cup, jug. The silhouette tell is the chipped lip — one small notch in the mouth of the vessel. It's the detail that says "this has been used."

Four poses

  • base — empty vessel at rest.
  • hold — something peeking out the top.
  • tip — rotated to pour (nothing coming out yet).
  • empty — upside down, eyes closed.

What to preserve

  • Chipped lip — silhouette tell.
  • Redwood cap — the one saturated colour on Jugs.
  • Slow-blink motion (Jugs is the stillest of the kid register).

Invitations

  • Could the body suggest both plastic (bottle) and ceramic (jug)? The character hosts both materially.
  • The label rectangle on the front is where our brand could show up subtly — invite your read.

Crate

rigid · box · the stack rhyme
cardboard #c99460 cardboard-dk #a07040 redwood arrow #b15043 ink #241c1e

Identity

Crate stacks, contains, builds. Hosts: shoebox, cardboard box, lego brick, cereal box, tissue box, cube-ish anything. The silhouette tell is the fragile arrow — a tiny red "this way up" arrow drawn quickly on one face, as if stamped with a handheld.

Four poses

  • base — closed cube at rest.
  • contain — lid slightly lifted, something inside peeking.
  • stack — one Crate on top of another (or stacked-shape).
  • fort — inverted with a doorway cut.

What to preserve

  • Fragile arrow — silhouette tell.
  • Cardboard brown palette (Crate is the warmest brown of the cast).
  • Creases / fold lines visible on faces.

Invitations

  • Could Crate's face be expressive via the arrow rotation? (arrow up = content, arrow tilted = confused, etc.)
  • Explore tape or strap decorations that don't overpower the box.

Mud

malleable · the shape-shift rhyme
mud #5a3a28 mud-lt #7d5942 grass #3d7a4a ink #241c1e

Identity

Mud takes the shape of whatever touches it. Hosts: dirt, clay, dough, malleable anything. Unique among the cast in that Mud has sleepy eyes (half-closed crescents) rather than round open eyes — malleable matter is the stillest behaviour. The silhouette tell is the grass tufts: two small green sprouts rising from opposite edges.

Four poses

  • base — mound at rest, sleepy eyes.
  • shape — small bump from something pressed in.
  • mould — flattened by a hand (visible handprint).
  • stick — something stuck halfway in (a Twig perhaps?).

What to preserve

  • Sleepy eyes (the one character with this trait).
  • Grass tufts — silhouette tell.
  • Slow-breath motion (11s — the slowest of the seven).
  • Warm brown palette (Mud is the warmest of the cast).

Invitations

  • Texture: Mud could be clay, dough, modelling compound, or genuine earth. Does your illustration lean toward one?
  • When Mud "stick"-poses, consider that a Twig character is in the scene — cross-character cameos are a future possibility.

Drip

liquid · the flow rhyme
drip #5a9fc4 drip-lt #bde0f0 drip-dk #2b5e80 drip-deep #153a52

Identity

Drip flows, pools, pours, soaks. The youngest sibling — built last and the most ambitious technically (soak pose shows a bleed stain spreading across a sponge substrate). Hosts: water, glue, paint, ink, juice, milk. The silhouette tell is the gloss highlight — a small white crescent in the upper-left of the body in every pose, reading as "this is a liquid, not a solid."

Four poses

  • base — a single teardrop (the canonical drop of liquid).
  • pool — spread flat with surface reflections + satellite droplets.
  • pour — vertical stream into a catch-pool, bubbles rising.
  • soak — bleed stain spreading across a porous sponge substrate with spatter dots.

What to preserve

  • Gloss highlight — silhouette tell, present in every pose.
  • Cool blue palette (complements Mud's warm browns — they're sibling characters).
  • Undulation motion (6s wave-like body oscillation).

Invitations

  • Drip's mouth/expression under the eyes is new territory — we've been eyes-only, but consider if expression could emerge in pour and soak poses.
  • The soak pose is our most narratively ambitious — it shows Drip interacting with a sponge-like substrate. Invite your interpretation of how much of the sponge to draw.

Seven surfaces they touch today, and five we want to build next. Your illustrations need to hold up in all twelve contexts. The sizes and spaces matter — no character should read ambiguously at any scale we'll display them.

Open questions for your process

These are the specific decisions we'd love your read on before you dig too deep:

  1. Stroke weight consistency. Current placeholders use an ink-wobble filter (0.018 frequency, 2px displacement). Does your natural line weight lend itself to the same filter, or do you prefer to draw the wobble? Either works — we need to know so we don't double-apply.
  2. Texture vs. flat fill. Sago Mini-level texture is beautiful but can be noisy at 40px. What's your opinion on a tiered texture system (flat at thumbnail, textured at hero size)?
  3. Expression vocabulary. Right now only eyes do expression work. Are you comfortable keeping eyes as the only expressive element, or do you want to invite eyebrow/mouth variations?
  4. Adult register. The current muted-sand variants are rough — is a single colour family the right direction for the quieter voice, or would you rather explore a completely different palette system (e.g. desaturated version of each character's kid palette)?
  5. Cross-character scenes. Future work includes poses where characters interact (Cord knotted around Twig, Swatch draped over Crate). Do you want to design those as a set of dedicated illustrations, or compose them from the base character poses?
  6. Naming. Cord / Jugs / Twig / Swatch / Crate / Mud / Drip are working placeholders pending a naming session with the collective. If any name is holding you back visually, flag it — the name can change to match what you draw.
  7. Delivery format. We'll want SVG primarily (our render path is inline SVG). But we'd also love the original illustration files (Procreate, Illustrator, Fresco — whatever you work in) so we can pull alternate angles later.
