# Harbour Tile Image Prompts (DALL-E)

Regenerated 07 April 2026. One prompt per harbour app, 19 total. Each prompt has three layers: a 3-sentence description of the app's mechanic and purpose, a visual architecture, and a style/palette block.

**Shared style baseline** (paste at the top of any DALL-E run for consistency):

> Bright daylight palette: warm cream background (#fdf6e8), with accents in soft turquoise (#7dd3c0), warm coral (#ff8a65), butter yellow (#ffd54f), and sky blue (#90caf9). Playful but not cartoony — illustrated editorial style with clean linework, gentle gradients, and subtle paper-grain texture. Square composition. No text, no UI chrome, no logos. Avoid cluttered or dark scenes.

---

## 1. creaseworks
**What it does:** Creaseworks is a creative-playdates platform where adults book short, structured sessions of analogue making (paper craft, drawing, collage) as a way to reconnect with playful learning. Users browse "packs" of activities, schedule sessions with friends, and share photos of what they made. The threshold concept is that *play is a discipline, not a regression* — practising it on purpose changes how you approach problems everywhere else.

**Visual architecture:** Three folded paper cranes mid-flight above an open notebook on a sunlit table. The notebook page shows a half-finished pencil sketch and a scatter of coloured pencil shavings. Soft window light from the upper left casts gentle shadows.

**Style & palette:** Cream paper background, cranes in coral and turquoise, pencils in butter yellow and sky blue. Clean editorial illustration with subtle paper grain. Light, airy, joyful.

## 2. vertigo.vault
**What it does:** Vertigo.vault is a curated library of learning activities tagged by skill, age, group size, and materials. Educators and facilitators search the vault, save activities to collections, and remix them into their own session plans. The threshold concept is that *good activities are recombinable building blocks*, not finished recipes — the same exercise can serve wildly different learning outcomes depending on context.

**Visual architecture:** A tall apothecary cabinet with many small drawers, each slightly ajar. Coloured paper cards spill out of two drawers and float gently upward, sorted by hue. A magnifying glass rests in front of the cabinet.

**Style & palette:** Pale wood cabinet against cream wall. Cards in turquoise, coral, butter yellow, and sky blue. Soft top-down lighting. Editorial illustration with clean lines and a touch of warmth.

## 3. depth.chart
**What it does:** Depth.chart is an assessment generator that turns the PRME framework's five threshold concepts into custom rubrics, prompts, and reflection questions for educators. Users select learning outcomes, depth level, and context, and the tool generates a printable assessment package. The threshold concept is that *assessment design is itself a teaching act* — the questions you ask shape what students believe is worth knowing.

**Visual architecture:** A nautical depth-sounding chart unfurled on a desk, with delicate contour lines forming concentric ridges. A brass plumb line dangles from above, casting a thin shadow across the lines. Three small flag markers stand at different depths.

**Style & palette:** Cream chart paper with soft turquoise contour lines, coral flags, butter yellow plumb line. Editorial illustration with gentle ink work and a paper-fold crease running diagonally. Bright and inviting.

## 4. deep.deck
**What it does:** Deep.deck is a digital deck of conversation cards designed to surface assumptions, surface tensions, and unlock honest dialogue in workshops or 1:1s. Users draw cards by theme (trust, power, change, identity) and the deck scaffolds the conversation with follow-ups. The threshold concept is that *good questions are infrastructure* — they create the conditions for thinking, they don't extract answers from people.

**Visual architecture:** A loose fan of oversized playing cards spread across a tabletop, each card showing a different abstract symbol (a spiral, a knot, an open hand, a doorway). The top card stands upright, balanced on its edge.

**Style & palette:** Cream tabletop, cards in alternating turquoise and coral with butter yellow symbols. Soft side lighting. Editorial illustration with crisp card edges and a subtle paper texture.

## 5. raft.house
**What it does:** Raft.house is a group-learning sandbox where small teams collaboratively build "rafts" — shared knowledge structures — by contributing pieces in real time. Each member adds a plank (a fact, claim, or question) and the group must keep the raft balanced as it grows. The threshold concept is that *collective understanding has structural integrity* — knowledge built together has different load-bearing properties than knowledge accumulated individually.

**Visual architecture:** A wooden raft made of mismatched planks, floating on calm turquoise water. Three small paper sails rise from the raft, each tied to a different plank. A line of bubbles trails behind it.

**Style & palette:** Cream sky and pale turquoise water, raft in warm wood tones, sails in coral, butter yellow, and sky blue. Editorial illustration with gentle ripples and crisp shadow under the raft. Sunny, optimistic.

## 6. tidal.pool
**What it does:** Tidal.pool is a systems-thinking sandbox where users tweak variables in a small ecosystem (a tide pool with a handful of species) and watch downstream effects ripple over generations. The mechanic teaches feedback loops, stocks-and-flows, and time-delayed consequences. The threshold concept is that *systems behave non-linearly* — small upstream changes can produce surprising downstream effects, and intuition trained on linear cause-and-effect actively misleads us.

**Visual architecture:** A top-down view of a small rocky tide pool with three or four sea creatures (a starfish, an anemone, a tiny fish), surrounded by ripples that radiate outward in concentric rings. A single droplet falls from above, frozen mid-fall.

**Style & palette:** Cream rocks, turquoise water, coral starfish, butter yellow anemone, sky blue ripples. Editorial illustration with soft watercolour washes and crisp linework on the creatures.

## 7. paper.trail
**What it does:** Paper.trail bridges physical and digital learning by letting users scan handwritten notes, sketches, or paper artifacts and link them to digital activities, reflections, or assessment items. The mechanic preserves the texture of analogue thinking inside a searchable archive. The threshold concept is that *the medium of capture shapes the thought itself* — handwriting, doodling, and paper artefacts produce different kinds of ideas than typing ever will.

**Visual architecture:** A trail of small paper notes pinned along a clothesline, each note showing a different doodle or scrap of handwriting. A wooden clothespin holds the centre note slightly askew. Three more notes drift in the breeze just beyond the line.

**Style & palette:** Cream sky, butter yellow line, notes in pale turquoise, coral, and sky blue. Editorial illustration with playful hand-drawn marks on each note and soft ground shadows.

## 8. mirror.log
**What it does:** Mirror.log is a structured reflection journal that prompts users with progressive questions after a learning event, conversation, or workshop. Entries are private but can be shared selectively for peer reflection. The threshold concept is that *reflection is not the same as remembering* — it requires a deliberate change of perspective, a "mirror move" that lets you see your own thinking from outside.

**Visual architecture:** An open hand-mirror lying face-up on an open journal, with the mirror's reflection showing not the ceiling but a calm sky with one cloud. A fountain pen rests beside the journal, cap off, ink slightly bleeding into the page.

**Style & palette:** Cream journal pages, mirror frame in warm coral, sky reflection in pale turquoise and sky blue, pen in butter yellow. Editorial illustration with soft shadows and a subtle ink-bloom on the page.

## 9. orbit.lab
**What it does:** Orbit.lab is an interactive sandbox where users place a planet, set an orbiter's launch angle and velocity, and watch the resulting trajectory loop, escape, or spiral inward. The mechanic teaches gravitational mechanics through direct manipulation rather than equations. The threshold concept is that *orbits are falls that miss* — once you internalise that "in orbit" means "continuously falling around something," the entire conceptual scaffolding of celestial mechanics clicks into place.

**Visual architecture:** A small turquoise planet at centre, with a single orbiter drawing a delicate elliptical trail around it. A second, fainter trail shows an earlier failed attempt that escaped to the right. Three tiny stars dot the background.

**Style & palette:** Cream space background (not black — bright and airy), planet in turquoise, orbiter and trails in coral, secondary trail in butter yellow. Editorial illustration with clean curves and subtle motion blur on the orbiter.

## 10. proof.garden
**What it does:** Proof.garden lets users build mathematical proofs by dragging axiom and lemma "seeds" into a tree structure, with each step validated as they connect. Completed proofs grow visible branches; broken proofs wilt gently and prompt revision. The threshold concept is that *a proof is a path, not a verdict* — mathematics rewards the construction of an argument, not the recognition of a true statement.

**Visual architecture:** A small potted plant whose stems and branches form a clear tree diagram, each leaf labelled with a tiny abstract symbol. New buds at the top of the plant glow faintly. A watering can sits beside the pot.

**Style & palette:** Cream background, terracotta pot in warm coral, leaves in turquoise and sky blue, buds in butter yellow. Editorial botanical illustration with clean linework and gentle gradients on the leaves.

## 11. bias.lens
**What it does:** Bias.lens walks users through three interactive scenarios — one anchoring, one confirmation, one sunk-cost — letting them make choices, then revealing how their reasoning was shaped by the cognitive shortcut. Each scenario ends with a meta-reflection prompt. The threshold concept is that *biases aren't errors to be corrected, they're features of fast cognition* — you can't think your way out of them, only design environments that compensate for them.

**Visual architecture:** A pair of round eyeglasses lying on a cream surface, with each lens showing a slightly different distorted version of the same simple scene (three coloured shapes). One lens stretches the shapes; the other tints them.

**Style & palette:** Cream background, glasses frames in coral, lenses with subtle turquoise and butter yellow tints, shapes in sky blue and coral. Editorial illustration with clean glass highlights and soft ground shadows.

## 12. scale.shift
**What it does:** Scale.shift is a zoom-slider experience that takes users from atomic scale through cellular, human, planetary, and galactic scales in a single continuous gesture. At each level, the same visual element transforms into a recognisable counterpart at the new scale. The threshold concept is that *scale is not just bigger and smaller* — phenomena at different scales obey different rules, and intuitions trained at human scale fail dramatically at the extremes.

**Visual architecture:** A series of five concentric circles nested inside each other, each containing a different recognisable form (a hexagon, a leaf, a hand, a planet, a spiral galaxy). A subtle dotted line spirals outward through all five.

**Style & palette:** Cream background, concentric circles in alternating turquoise, coral, butter yellow, sky blue, and pale turquoise. Editorial illustration with clean concentric geometry and a delicate spiral overlay.

## 13. pattern.weave
**What it does:** Pattern.weave presents three gestalt-perception puzzles where users click to reveal hidden figures inside ambiguous compositions (figure-ground reversal, closure, common fate). Each reveal animates the shift from "noise" to "signal." The threshold concept is that *perception is constructed, not received* — what looks like "just looking" is actually an active process of pattern-completion, and once you can see how it works, you can't unsee it.

**Visual architecture:** A woven textile fragment where the warp threads form one image (a bird) and the weft threads form another (a fish), with the eye able to flip between the two depending on focus. A loose thread curls off the edge.

**Style & palette:** Cream backing fabric, warp threads in turquoise, weft threads in coral, accent threads in butter yellow and sky blue. Editorial illustration with detailed thread texture and crisp edges.

## 14. market.mind
**What it does:** Market.mind is a two-resource allocation sandbox where users distribute time and attention between competing goals, with a "ghost overlay" that visualises the opportunity cost of every decision. The mechanic makes invisible trade-offs visible. The threshold concept is that *every choice is also a refusal* — the cost of an action isn't what you pay, it's the next-best thing you could have done with the same resources.

**Visual architecture:** A balance scale with two pans, each holding a small stack of coloured tokens. A faint ghostly outline of additional tokens hovers above each pan, showing what *could* have been placed there. The fulcrum is gently tilted.

**Style & palette:** Cream background, scale in warm coral, real tokens in turquoise and butter yellow, ghost tokens in pale sky blue with low opacity. Editorial illustration with crisp metal highlights on the scale.

## 15. rhythm.lab
**What it does:** Rhythm.lab is a 4×4 step-sequencer that lets users place beats, accents, and subdivisions on a grid and hear the pattern loop in real time. Different cells map to different drum and percussion sounds, and the user can layer polyrhythms. The threshold concept is that *rhythm is felt subdivision, not counting* — once you stop counting beats and start feeling the spaces between them, time itself becomes elastic.

**Visual architecture:** A 4×4 grid of round buttons, with about half lit up in different colours, arranged in a clearly rhythmic pattern. Three small concentric rings emanate from one of the lit buttons as if a sound just played.

**Style & palette:** Cream grid background, lit buttons in coral, turquoise, butter yellow, and sky blue, unlit buttons in pale cream-grey. Editorial illustration with subtle button highlights and clean geometric layout.

## 16. code.weave
**What it does:** Code.weave is a recursion-tree visualiser that takes a small recursive function (factorial, Fibonacci, mergesort) and animates the call stack unfolding, branching, and collapsing back. Users step through the tree at their own pace. The threshold concept is that *recursion is not repetition* — it's a structural relationship between a problem and a smaller version of itself, and seeing the tree makes that relationship suddenly tangible instead of dizzying.

**Visual architecture:** A delicate tree-diagram that branches downward, with each node connected to two smaller children, fading from solid colour at the top to soft sketch at the bottom. A single highlighted path runs from root to one leaf.

**Style & palette:** Cream background, tree nodes in turquoise circles, branches in coral, highlighted path in butter yellow, leaves in sky blue. Editorial illustration with clean geometric branching and soft node shadows.

## 17. time.prism
**What it does:** Time.prism places users inside a single historical moment with three decision points, presenting only the information actually available at the time — no hindsight, no foreshadowing. After choosing, the user sees what happened and reflects on what they couldn't have known. The threshold concept is that *historical empathy requires giving up hindsight* — judging the past by what we now know is the opposite of understanding it.

**Visual architecture:** A triangular glass prism on a cream surface, splitting a single beam of warm light into three coloured rays that fan outward. Each ray ends at a small hand-drawn icon (a key, a letter, a compass). A faint dust-mote floats in the beam.

**Style & palette:** Cream background, prism with crisp glass highlights, rays in coral, butter yellow, and turquoise, icons in sky blue. Editorial illustration with delicate light refraction and a soft cast shadow under the prism.

## 18. liminal.pass
**What it does:** Liminal.pass is a series of three "aha-moment" puzzles drawn from different domains (mathematics, language, perception), each followed by a meta-reflection on what shifted in the user's thinking when the answer clicked. The puzzles are designed so the *moment* of understanding is the lesson. The threshold concept is — recursively — *threshold concepts themselves*: ideas you can't go back from, that reorganise everything else you know about a domain.

**Visual architecture:** A simple stone archway standing alone in an open space, with light streaming through it from the far side. The ground on the near side is muted; the ground visible through the archway is brighter and more colourful, as if a different world begins on the other side.

**Style & palette:** Cream sky, archway in warm coral stone, near-side ground in muted sky blue, far-side ground glowing in butter yellow and turquoise. Editorial illustration with soft volumetric light through the archway.

## 19. emerge.box
**What it does:** Emerge.box is an interactive Conway's Game of Life — users click cells on a grid to seed an initial pattern, then press play and watch complex behaviour arise from three simple rules. Save, share, and remix patterns with the community. The threshold concept is that *complexity emerges from simplicity* — you cannot predict the macro behaviour of a system from inspecting its rules, no matter how well you understand them, and that gap is where most of the interesting science lives.

**Visual architecture:** A grid of small square cells, with about a quarter of them lit up in a pattern that looks midway through evolving — recognisable shapes (a glider, a blinker, a small cluster) dotted across the field. A faint trail shows where one shape just moved.

**Style & palette:** Cream grid background, lit cells in turquoise, coral, butter yellow, and sky blue, with the moving glider trail in pale coral. Editorial illustration with crisp cell edges and a subtle drop-shadow on the whole grid.

## Notes on use

- **Generation order if time/credits are tight:** start with the four flagship apps (creaseworks, vertigo.vault, depth.chart, deep.deck), then the three "live mechanics" (orbit.lab, emerge.box, rhythm.lab), then the rest in any order.
- **Output dimensions:** generate at 1024×1024 square. The harbour hub crops to a card aspect ratio at display time, so keep critical elements in the centre 70%.
- **Naming convention:** save each as `apps/harbour/public/images/{slug}.png` (e.g. `creaseworks.png`, `vertigo-vault.png`). The harbour hub's `notion.ts` already references these paths.
- **Iteration:** if a generated image lands too dark or too cartoony, the most reliable lever is to re-emphasise *"editorial illustration, cream background, soft daylight, no heavy outlines"* in the prompt — DALL-E tends to drift toward saturated cartoon styles otherwise.
