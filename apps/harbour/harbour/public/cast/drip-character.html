drip sketch — v1

  • Archetype: the adaptive one. Validates "it's okay to let the situation shape you." Drip is the character who doesn't fight its context — it fills whatever vessel holds it.
  • Form: liquid. Hosts DB form_primary values like liquid, water, paint, ink, glue, slime, juice, milk.
  • Motion vocabulary: fluid, wave-like oscillation. .flow runs a 6-second cycle of scale (±1.5%) and rotation (±0.4°) — the body breathes like surface tension settling. Slightly faster and wavier than mud's slowshift (11s, subtler) because liquid is more mobile than malleable solid. The .wink eye animation fires every 5s with a quick scaleY squash. Stagger variants (.a, .b, .c) keep the tiles from flowing in lockstep.
  • Silhouette rule: the gloss highlight is the tell. Every pose carries a white cartoon-water crescent in the upper-left body area — silhouette-level signature that says "this is a liquid, not a solid." Applied with --gloss at 0.75–0.78 opacity. The crescent's orientation and size adapts per pose but its function (catches light on the body's shoulder) stays constant.
  • Secondary silhouette signals: satellite droplets around pool (small teardrops free-floating near the main body), bubbles in the pour (small highlighted circles rising through the stream), spatter dots across the soak substrate (dry drip marks around the bleed stain). These amplify "this is liquid" without being the primary tell.
  • Palette: --drip #5a9fc4 (cornflower water blue, body), --drip-lt #bde0f0 (pale cyan top-light), --drip-dk #2b5e80 (shadow), --drip-deep #153a52 (deepest shadow + ground-contact), --gloss #ffffff (the tell). Cool blues complement mud's warm browns — liquid vs earth.
  • Eyes: round + cream catchlight (same pattern as Cord/Jugs/Twig/Swatch/Crate — not sleepy like Mud). The double catchlight reinforces the "sparkly water" feel. Eyes sit in the body's lower round section on each pose. .wink animation is a quick scaleY dip — faster than Jugs's slowblink, same pattern as Cord's blink.
  • Function poses: base (single teardrop, narrow top → round bottom — the canonical "drop of liquid"), pool (spread out flat with surface reflections + satellite droplets — "liquid takes the shape of its container"), pour (vertical stream descending into a catch-pool, with bubbles rising and a next-drop forming at the top — "liquid transferred"), soak (watercolour bleed stain spreading across a porous sponge substrate — concentric bloom rings + dry spatter dots — "liquid absorbed into another material"). The four-pose arc reads as a lifecycle: drop → pool → pour → soak → back to drop.
  • Adult mode: flat rendering, muted sand palette, no wobble filter. Tiny gloss highlight remains (the tell) but at 0.55 opacity so it's present-but-quiet. Round open eyes (no catchlights, no winking) — adult drip reads as focused rather than sparkly.
  • Relationship to mud: they're sibling characters. Mud is malleable-solid (stays pressed), Drip is liquid (takes the shape but doesn't hold it). Their poses echo each other (puddle/pool, shape/drop) but their motion vocabularies are opposite — Mud is stillness-adjacent (11s slow breath), Drip is wave-like motion (6s undulation).
