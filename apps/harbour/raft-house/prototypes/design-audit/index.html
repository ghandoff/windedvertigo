systemic issues

problems that repeat across multiple games — fix these at the system level, not game by game.

academic voice in participant prompts

35 of 45 games use academic sentence structures: "the threshold concept reveals that..." / "what does this teach about the relationship between local correctness and global correctness?" kids don't talk or think like journal articles.

fix: rewrite all prompts in conversational second-person. "what do you notice?" beats "describe the emergent properties of the system." test every prompt by reading it aloud — if it sounds like a textbook, rewrite.

reflection prompts assume adult life experience

nearly every reflection asks participants to connect to "your work," "a project you're designing," "your current workflow." kids don't have workflows or design projects. these prompts will produce blank stares.

fix: create age-adaptive prompt variants. for ages 10-14: "think about a time at school when..." / "imagine you and your friends are..." keep adult versions as facilitator-selectable options.

reveal prompts lecture instead of land

reveal prompts often read like mini-lectures: "the threshold: mathematical truth is not monolithic." this is telling, not crossing. 28 of 45 reveals are expositions.

fix: rewrite reveals as questions or provocations. "wait — so the same statement can be true in one system and false in another?" let the participant's own confusion become the insight.

jargon introduced without scaffolding

terms like "cantor's diagonal argument," "coffman conditions," "overjustification effect," "le chatelier's principle," "hermeneutic circle" appear with no glossary or simpler explanation. 127 such terms across all games.

fix: add a hint system. each jargon term gets a tap-to-reveal explanation. or rewrite the prompt to not need the term. "the principle that reactions push back against changes" teaches le chatelier without naming it.

asymmetric role descriptions are walls of text

every asymmetric activity has 3-4 roles with 3-4 sentences of dense info. on a phone, this is 6+ lines of tiny text. kids will skim or skip.

fix: cap role descriptions at 2 short sentences + 1 bold key fact. consider audio narration as an alternative.

minimum text lengths on reflections

most reflections require 50-80 character minimums. for kids who struggle with writing fluency, this creates anxiety. the character counter becomes a "not good enough yet" signal.

fix: remove minLength for younger audiences. replace with encouraging prompts: "want to add more?" voice input could transform reflections for reluctant writers.

time pressure may cause anxiety

14 games have time limits under 60 seconds on predictions. timed activities can trigger anxiety in younger or neurodivergent participants.

fix: make timers facilitator-optional, not baked into the activity. show "take your time" instead of countdown for younger groups.

no visual/spatial activities for kinesthetic learners

canvas (2D pin placement) is the only spatial interaction. all other activities are text-heavy: read, think, type. the original prototype had gesture-based interaction — that was lost in conversion.

fix: expand drawing mode in open-response. add drag-and-drop puzzle pieces. consider "sketch your thinking" alongside every text reflection.

priority matrix

what to fix first, based on impact and effort.

high impact, low effort — do first

  • rewrite reflection prompts: school/friend/game contexts instead of "your work"
  • remove minLength on reflections (or make facilitator-configurable)
  • simplify reveal prompts — question format instead of lecture
  • add age-level selector to discover page

high impact, high effort — plan for

  • create kid-language variants of all 45 games
  • add tap-to-explain glossary for 127 jargon terms
  • audio narration for role descriptions
  • voice input for reflections (speech-to-text)

low impact, low effort — batch fix

  • make timers facilitator-optional
  • increase canvas grid line opacity (10% to 20%)
  • enlarge sorting category pill touch targets
  • add emoji to phase labels

low impact, high effort — nice to have

  • drag-and-drop puzzle pieces
  • animated data visualizations for reveals
  • per-discipline visual themes
  • sketch/drawing mode for all reflections
