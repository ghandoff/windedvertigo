# Audience Segments

## K–12 Educators

**Who they are**
Classroom teachers (grades K–12), curriculum leads, school librarians, art/design specialists in public and independent schools. Usually 10–25 years in role. Seeking practical, implementable tools for student-centred learning.

**Pain points**
- Curriculum crowding: too many mandates, not enough time
- Limited professional development budget and time
- Assessment pressure (standardised testing) vs. desire to teach for depth
- Lack of peer networks in their school; professional isolation
- Imposter syndrome around "modern" teaching methods
- Tech adoption fatigue (LMS, tools that don't integrate)

**Where they engage**
- Pinterest, Teachers Pay Teachers, #edutwitter (some), Bluesky edu community
- Conference presentations (ASCD, NSTA, regional teacher associations)
- WhatsApp/Facebook groups (subject-specific, regional)
- Email newsletters (curated teaching resources)
- School staff meetings, professional learning communities

**What content resonates**
- Case studies: "I tried this in my Year 5 class and here's what happened"
- Resource templates they can immediately adapt
- Evidence that this works for *their* subjects (maths, English, science, design)
- Peer testimonials; teacher-to-teacher advice
- Short video clips (5–10 min) of techniques in action
- Honest challenges: "This didn't work until I..."

**w.v products that map**
- **creaseworks**: Creative confidence toolkit, design thinking for any subject
- **whirlpool**: Community learning events (some free, some paid professional development)
- Depth-chart (within harbour): Evidence, lesson plans, implementation scaffolds
- Play & Fair labs: Research on play-based learning, reportable outcomes

**Acquisition strategy**
- Partner with teacher networks (ASCD, regional ed associations)
- Direct outreach to curriculum leads
- Grassroots: free resources on Teachers Pay Teachers → email capture
- Collaborate with Press Play on co-hosted whirlpool sessions targeting educators

## Higher Ed Faculty

**Who they are**
Tenured and early-career faculty in education, business schools, design schools, international development, psychology. Usually 5–20 years post-PhD. Teaching 100–300 students/semester. Under pressure to innovate pedagogy while maintaining research credibility.

**Pain points**
- Tension between content coverage and depth
- Large lecture halls and passive learning cultures
- Limited time to redesign courses; pressure to "add" rather than "redesign"
- Accreditation requirements (AACSB, PRME) without clear implementation roadmap
- Uneven student engagement and retention
- Scepticism about "edtech solutions" that oversimplify learning
- Limited budget for pedagogy consulting; reluctant to outsource

**Where they engage**
- Academic conferences (AERA, AASHE, subject-specific)
- Higher ed journals and newsletters (The Chronicle, Inside Higher Ed)
- Email (thoughtful, evidence-based content)
- LinkedIn (especially business/design faculty)
- Peer referral and professional networks
- Institutional partnerships (ESOs, learning labs)

**What content resonates**
- Research papers, policy briefs, evidence synthesis on pedagogy
- "How we redesigned [course/programme] using..." case studies
- Toolkit for PRME compliance with evidence infrastructure
- Peer networks: "Here's how faculty at 5 universities implemented this"
- Accreditation alignment (AACSB, PRME positioning)
- Honest about trade-offs: implementation time, resource needs

**w.v products that map**
- **PPCS** (PRME Pedagogy Certificate System): Evidence infrastructure, AR programme
- **Whirlpool**: Professional learning, faculty seminars
- **Depth-chart**: Lesson design, assessment frameworks
- Research outputs: Play & Fair labs, Perpetual labs

**Acquisition strategy**
- PRME member outreach (direct to Meredith's network)
- Conference presentations (AERA, AASHE, subject-specific)
- Institutional partnerships (sell to provosts, learning centres)
- Email campaigns focused on accreditation compliance + research credibility

## Parents & Families

**Who they are**
Parents aged 25–55, across income levels and geographies. Some homeschooling, some in traditional schools. Seeking developmental understanding and practical strategies. Increasingly interested in SEL, play-based learning, digital wellbeing. Often anxious about "what's normal?"

**Pain points**
- Overwhelm: conflicting parenting advice, Instagram mom culture
- Guilt about screen time, outdoor play, structured vs. unstructured time
- Confusion about child development (what's age-appropriate?)
- Limited support networks (geographically scattered families)
- Pressure to optimise learning; fear of "falling behind"
- Desire to support play and creativity without being passive

**Where they engage**
- Instagram, TikTok (quick wins, relatability)
- Email newsletters (parenting advice, development insights)
- Podcast (commute, exercise listening)
- Facebook groups (local parent networks, topic-specific)
- Schools (parent workshops, newsletters)
- Direct peer networks and playgroups

**What content resonates**
- "What to expect at age 3, 7, 12" developmental guides
- Short-form video: "5-minute activities for rainy days"
- Podcast episodes: honest parent conversations
- Parent testimonials: "We tried this and our kid's creativity bloomed"
- Permission to let kids play without constant instruction
- Evidence-based debunking: "Actually, boredom is good"
- Accessible, non-judgmental tone

**w.v products that map**
- **creaseworks**: At-home creative activities, confidence building
- **eddyy**: Early learning exploration (if productised)
- **Play & Fair labs**: Research on play, shared with parents
- **Whirlpool**: Parent-focused sessions (maybe quarterly)
- Email courses: "Play & Learning for Your [Age Group]"

**Acquisition strategy**
- Instagram organic + partnerships with parent influencers
- Email capture via free downloadable guides ("50 Rainy Day Activities")
- Parent workshops at schools and libraries
- Podcast guest spots (parenting, child development shows)
- Press Play collaboration on family-focused whirlpools

## Learning Designers & Instructional Designers

**Who they are**
Corporate L&D professionals, educational technologists, eLearning developers, freelance instructional designers. 3–15 years in field. Building courses, programmes, and learning experiences across sectors (corporate, NGO, government, higher ed).

**Pain points**
- Pressure to deliver fast (ADDIE to rapid prototyping)
- Balancing engagement with measurable outcomes
- Limited time for research and theory; most work is pragmatic
- Isolation: freelancers especially lack peer networks
- Imposter syndrome: "Am I doing this right?"
- Tools that don't integrate; data silos

**Where they engage**
- LinkedIn (professional community, job search)
- Substack and Medium (practitioner voices)
- Learning Design conferences (eLearning Guild, IDOL, subject-specific)
- Email newsletters (industry insights)
- Slack communities (eLearning Guild, local chapters)
- Webinars and online workshops

**What content resonates**
- Practical methodologies: "Here's our design process"
- Tool reviews and templates
- Case studies with metrics: "We increased completion 23% by..."
- Evidence-based design principles with theory
- Peer stories: other designers sharing challenges and solutions
- Thought leadership on emerging topics (AI, neuroscience, async)
- Community: connection with other designers

**w.v products that map**
- **creaseworks**: Design methodology, creativity in learning
- **Depth-chart**: Learning design framework, assessment scaffolds
- **PPCS**: Evidence infrastructure for impact measurement
- **Play & Fair labs**: Research inputs for design
- **Harbour**: Apps as design exemplars

**Acquisition strategy**
- LinkedIn thought leadership posts (Garrett, Lamis)
- Substack: deep dives on learning design methodology
- Conference presentations and sponsorships (eLearning Guild, IDOL)
- Email newsletter for LD community
- Slack community seeding (guilds, local chapters)
- Freemium: design templates, assessment frameworks

## EdTech Communities & Product Managers

**Who they are**
EdTech founders, product managers, UX researchers, engineers in learning tech. Building tools, platforms, and services. Seeking partnership, distribution, thought leadership alignment.

**Pain points**
- Competing on features, not pedagogy
- Integration hell: schools have 50 tools, none talk to each other
- Proof of learning impact; hard to show ROI
- User acquisition costs and churn
- Talent: hiring learning designers and researchers is hard
- Funding pressure: need defensible positioning

**Where they engage**
- LinkedIn (professional identity, hiring)
- EdSurge, EdTech news outlets
- Industry conferences (ISTE, SXSW Edu, AngelHack Ed)
- VCs and demo day networks
- Slack communities (EdTech founders, product managers)
- Direct partnerships and integrations

**What content resonates**
- Product strategy and design philosophy
- Evidence: "Here's the learning research behind our feature"
- Partnership opportunities
- Emerging tools and integrations
- Thought leadership on the future of learning tech
- Founder stories: "How we built this"
- Honest product critique

**w.v products that map**
- **Harbour**: Suite of integrated apps (creaseworks, depth-chart, eddyy)
- **PPCS**: Integration with learning platforms
- Research outputs: foundational thinking for product roadmaps
- Speaking/thought leadership

**Acquisition strategy**
- LinkedIn and conference presence (Garrett's thought leadership)
- Direct partnerships (port CRM, integration conversations)
- Speaking at ISTE, EdSurge events
- Substack thought leadership
- Product showcases at whirlpool + conferences

## Institutional Buyers (UN, Foundations, Government)

**Who they are**
Programme officers at UN agencies (UNICEF, Global Compact), foundations (Gates, Omidyar, regional), government ministries (education, international development), development banks (IDB). Decision-making by committee; 6–18 month procurement cycles.

**Pain points**
- Impact measurement and attribution
- Scale vs. depth: how to reach marginalised populations
- Vendor fatigue: many vendors, unclear differentiation
- Budget cycles and approval timelines
- Local context variation (rural/urban, language, digital access)
- Risk aversion: proven solutions preferred

**Where they engage**
- RFP processes and vendor platforms
- Direct relationship management and proposal conversations
- Sector reports and evidence briefs
- Convenings and working groups
- Email (formal communications)
- Referrals from peer organisations

**What content resonates**
- Impact evidence: randomised trials, quasi-experimental designs
- Cost-effectiveness and scalability analysis
- Sustainability and local capacity building
- Risk mitigation: "Here's how we de-risk implementation"
- Alignment with institutional goals (SDGs, PRME, etc.)
- Case studies from similar contexts
- Technical documentation and process guides

**w.v products that map**
- **PPCS**: UN Global Compact partner, evidence infrastructure (PRME contract live)
- **Harbour + Depth-chart**: Implementation toolkit for government rollout
- **Play & Fair labs**: Research evidence
- IDB Salvador engagement: ed-tech procurement, MINEDUCYT alignment

**Acquisition strategy**
- Proactive relationship management (Garrett + Maria)
- RFP responses and technical proposals
- Evidence reports and impact briefs (published)
- Conferences and convenings (World Economic Forum, UN learning events)
- Partner referrals (other NGOs, consultancies)
- Existing relationships (Meredith at Global Compact, UN learning networks)
