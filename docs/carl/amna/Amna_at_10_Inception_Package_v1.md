## 1. Headline picture — what we know, where to aim

### The evidence base is substantial and well-organised

Amna has handed us **3,923 files** across three top-level corpora: *Amna Programmes* (11 pillar MEAL folders), *Amna Strategies and processes reports* (15 strategic spine documents), and *Evaluations* (11 past + 4 ongoing external/internal evaluations). The base spans 2016–2026 across seven displacement regions (Greece, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Jordan, Lebanon, Ukraine, Palestine/Gaza, plus dispersed contexts). The bulk of the corpus is .docx (1,286), .xlsx (793), .pdf (685), with substantial visual evidence (~750 photos, ~60 videos) and 276 .pptx training/strategy materials.

### Amna's own theory is unusually well-articulated

Across four foundational spine documents — the 2022 Yousafzai/Zanial 6-year evaluation (Harvard T.H. Chan SPH), the 2025 *Strategic Evolution* deck, the 2026 *Healing Ecosystems Internal Narrative*, and the 2025-2028 *MEL TOC* — Amna names its model with care, consistency, and an unusually high tolerance for surfacing tensions. **The synthesis does not need to construct Amna's theory of change. It needs to honour, test, and extend the theory Amna has already articulated.**

### Five spine themes will likely become our analytical anchors

1. **Identity-informed, not trauma alone.** Healing runs through dignity, belonging, culture, language, race, religion, migration experience — not symptom reduction.
2. **The cascade-vs-relational-depth tension.** Amna repeatedly names the risk that scaling weakens depth; this is the central ethical tension of the next decade.
3. **Implicit → explicit systems work.** Amna frames the strategic pivot as making conscious what was already happening; the synthesis must interrogate whether this continuity is real or retrofitted.
4. **What outlasts the grant.** Amna's strongest strategic claim is that the most durable impact lies in capacity, culture, relationships, and workforce — not single programmes. This is the most demanding evidentiary claim in the retrospective.
5. **Honest limitation-naming as cultural signature.** Even Amna's external decks name risks plainly. This is itself a finding — and a feature we should honour, not edit out.

### The synthesis's contribution to the MHPSS field is clearer than the TOR alone suggests

The strongest **field-positioning argument** is not "Amna is alternative" or "Amna is complementary" — it is **Amna is what the field has been arguing for**. The most rigorous quantitative work in MHPSS (Kohrt's ENACT, Singla et al. 2017 on relational common factors) and the most rigorous critical work (Mills, Summerfield, Fernando, Cosgrove et al. 2020) converge on relational, community-held, culturally grounded practice. Amna is the decade-long operational implementation of that convergence. The synthesis should make that argument explicit.

### Three things we'll need to negotiate at inception

- **What "primary evidence" means in this synthesis.** We've committed to weighting Amna's internal learning (reflective logs, training feedback, partner reports) as primary — not secondary "grey." This needs methodological justification in the inception note and operational discipline in the appraisal logic.
- **How findings get validated.** RAMESES standards expect documented sense-making touchpoints with the commissioning organisation. We need to agree these with Hejer (MEL lead) at the kick-off — likely a kickoff alignment, IPT validation, mid-synthesis sense-making, and pre-finalisation review.
- **What gets locked in vs left flexible.** The initial programme theory, search corpus, and CMO inventory will evolve. Deliverable format, scope (11 pillars), timeline, ethics commitments, and authorship/voice protocols are fixed at inception. Naming this distinction protects both sides.

## 3. Methodological spine

### 3.1 Realist-informed thematic synthesis (not full realist review)

Our approach is **realist-informed** rather than full realist synthesis. The distinction matters and we should name it in the inception note: a full realist review (Pawson, Wong, Greenhalgh) is structured around iterative testing of a programme theory through purposive search; a realist-informed thematic synthesis uses **context–mechanism–outcome (CMO) configurations** as the analytical scaffold but blends with thematic synthesis methods appropriate to a heterogeneous evidence base where the programme theory is partly tacit and partly emergent.

We cite Pawson & Tilley (1997), Wong et al. (RAMESES, 2013), and Dalkin et al. (2015) on CMO articulation, while noting that the desk-review-only scope and the partnership-led evidence base require methodological pragmatism. We follow the spirit of RAMESES II reporting standards.

### 3.2 The Initial Programme Theory (IPT)

Before the inception note is finalised, we will draft a 2-page **Initial Programme Theory** sketch — provisional, marked as such. The IPT renders amna's "active ingredients" hypothesis as **6–12 candidate CMO conjectures**, each in the form:

> *In context C [partner organisation in protracted displacement, female-led staffing], mechanism M [validated reflective practice space] fires reasoning/resource R [practitioner sense of being held], producing outcome O [reduced practitioner burnout, sustained service continuity, capacity for attuned response to children]*

Each conjecture is tagged **tacit / documented / contested**. The IPT evolves through the synthesis — that's the point.

### 3.3 The Evidence & Claims Map (the synthesis's signature deliverable)

A 3ie/Campbell-style grid, but cells carry **claim status**, not just study counts. Pillars on the y-axis, outcome domains on the x-axis. Cells show:

- **Filled-circle confidence rating** (●●● well-supported / ●●○ emerging / ◑○○ promising-but-thin / ○○○ gap)
- **Bubble size** proportional to evidence item count
- **Bubble colour** showing source-mix (orange = external eval, blue = practitioner narrative, green = MEAL data, grey = training feedback)

Below the grid, a **horizontal source-mix transparency strip** per pillar — pre-empts the "where's your RCT?" question by making the evidence ecology visible and arguing it's *appropriate* to the claim type.

The map is paired with a **CERQual-style Summary of Findings table** in the Deep Learning Synthesis appendix, with confidence ratings (High/Moderate/Low/Very Low) and explicit "Safe claim language" column for the donor-facing brief:

| Confidence | Donor-brief language | Language to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| High / Well-supported | "Evidence consistently shows…" | "Proves," "causes," numerical effect claims |
| Moderate / Emerging | "Evidence suggests…" / "Practitioners and evaluators converge on…" | "Demonstrates," "shows that" |
| Low / Promising | "Early signals indicate…" | Generalising claims |
| Gap | "An open question for the next phase is…" | Silence — *name the gap, don't hide it* |

### 3.4 Treating practitioner evidence as primary, not "grey"

Methodological position to commit to in the inception note:

- Practitioner narratives, reflective practice logs, and training feedback are **first-class evidence for the mechanisms and relational processes they speak to** (caregiver–child connection, communities of practice as healing mechanism, accompaniment quality).
- External evaluations are first-class evidence for **reach, scale, and outcomes that benefit from outsider triangulation** (knowledge gains, distress reduction, organisational change).
- Where a claim involves both, both source types are weighted; where one is more appropriate, the asymmetry is named.

This is principled, not opportunistic — we cite Kothari et al. on uncovering tacit knowledge, ALNAP on frontline learning, and the broader practitioner-engaged evidence synthesis literature.

### 3.5 Anti-extractive epistemological commitments

Drawing on Tuck "Suspending Damage" (2009), Tuck & Yang "R-Words" (2014), Smith *Decolonizing Methodologies*, Chilisa *Indigenous Research Methodologies*, Wilson *Research is Ceremony*, and the Equitable Evaluation Framework, our synthesis pre-commits to:

1. **Live community-language register** of amna's outcome domains (safety, regulation, belonging, joy, agency, wellbeing, protective relationships, sense of future) in the languages amna actually uses. Translation moves are logged.
2. **First-pass coding frame sourced from amna learning logs and member voice**, only then layered with external literature. Every place where an external category overwrites a community one is logged.
3. **Provenance-tracked claims.** Each claim carries author / position / relationship metadata.
4. **Interpretation log.** Every collapse, re-naming, or generalisation gets a one-line audit entry.
5. **Surface tensions; refuse premature thematic resolution.** Where amna documents contradict each other or external literature, the contradiction is the finding.
6. **Pre-committed return points.** Sense-making with amna at: kick-off, IPT validation, mid-synthesis, draft review. Pushback treated as data.
7. **Refusal protocol.** Co-signed with amna naming what won't be extracted or published (identifying detail, sacred/ceremonial practice, internal political tensions).
8. **Citation justice.** Hand-search *Intervention*, *Transcultural Psychiatry*, MHPSS.net grey literature, Global South authors, lived-experience voices.

## 4. Amna's theory of practice (as Amna names it)

### 4.1 The L1–L4 ecosystem architecture

From the *Strategic Evolution* deck:

- **L1 — Community-Level Healing.** Direct training of partners' frontline workers (the historic core of amna's work).
- **L2 — Regional Master Trainers / Amna Fellows.** Cascade: training trainers within partner organisations and regional ecosystems.
- **L3 — Regional Healing Ecosystems.** Networks of practice — partners hold each other through communities of practice, peer learning, regional coordinators.
- **L4 — Global Healing Network.** Field-building, sustainability, methodology stewardship.

Operationalised via **Context-Anchored Ecosystems** with Regional Coordinators selected for "political, cultural, linguistic fluency" (per Internal Narrative 2026).

### 4.2 The Enter → Build → Sustain → Embed flow

From the MEL TOC:

- **Enter (Emergency Response).** Stabilise; equip frontline workers in acute response.
- **Build (Community Partnerships, including Baytna, Dinami, GCP).** Depth and durability over 1–3 years.
- **Sustain (Global Healing Network).** Continuity and shared leadership after grants end.
- **Embed (Systems of Care).** Vertical integration into ministries, schools, health systems, policy.

> *"These pathways are not rigid or one-way. We can enter at different points and loop back when contexts shift."* — MEL TOC

### 4.3 Active ingredients (as Amna names them)

From the 2026 Internal Narrative — the fullest statement we have:

> *trauma-informed practice; safe expression and gentle release of grief, shock, distress and overwhelm; identity-informed care; cultural wisdoms; collective healing; embodiment, play, reflection, creativity, joy reawakening, connection to self and others and community belonging*

From the 2022 evaluation (SWOT Table 1):

> *Culturally competent delivery personnel, community building (bridging divides in community, building social connections and peer support), safe spaces for freedom of expression and fostering agency, giving voice to individuals and communities, range of activities to provide psychosocial support (music, movement, art, storytelling)*

And the ecosystem-level "scaffold":

> *organisational accompaniment + communities of practice + (training + mentoring + reflective practice + tools + funding)*

### 4.4 Outcome domains (from TOR)

Eight: **safety, regulation, belonging, joy, agency, wellbeing, protective relationships, sense of future.**

These will live in the rows/columns of our Evidence & Claims Map. They are *amna's* outcome categories — we treat them as primary, not as categories to be mapped onto external taxonomies.

### 4.5 Open questions amna explicitly names (from the 2026 Internal Narrative)

These are live, named-by-amna questions the synthesis can speak directly to:

- What can partners realistically implement and sustain *without funding*?
- What is the minimum funding necessary for organisational embedding?
- What resources are needed for staff time, adaptation, supervision, integration?
- What are the **training selection criteria** (organisations vs individuals) for L2 cascade?
- What are the **readiness indicators, training/accompaniment/supervision packages, quality assurance, mentorship structures, compensation, sustainability, and ethical boundaries of cascade**?
- Does L2-first (as piloted in Gaza) work, or is sequential L1→L2 the necessary path?
- What creates **lasting healing capacity** vs. programme-bound impact?
- What is the role of **cultural wisdom** in healing ecosystems?
- What does **intergenerational trauma healing** look like in amna's model?

## 5. Field positioning frame

### 5.1 What to cite, what to critique, what to position around

**Cite (as field lingua franca):**

- **IASC Guidelines on MHPSS in Emergency Settings (2007)** — the field's shared vocabulary; cite respectfully. Note: the MHPSS Intervention Pyramid is structured by individual-tier need, while amna works in a relational ecology across tiers 2–3 simultaneously.
- **WHO mhGAP Humanitarian Intervention Guide (2015)** — the field's clinical edge case. Cite to demonstrate fluency.
- **IASC Common M&E Framework v2.0 (2021)** — the field's M&E reference point.

**Critique (the substantive frontier):**

- **Summerfield (1999, 2008)** — PTSD as Western export, empirical fragility of universalising diagnostic categories.
- **Mills (2014)** *Decolonizing Global Mental Health* — "scale-up" as psychiatric imperialism. Pair with **Mills & Fernando (2014)**.
- **Fernando (2014)** *Mental Health Worldwide*.
- **Jain & Jadhav (2009)** "Pills that Swallow Policy" — community psychiatry collapsing into pharmaceutical distribution.
- **White, Jain, Orr & Read (2017)** *Palgrave Handbook of Sociocultural Perspectives on Global Mental Health* — anchor anthology.
- **Cosgrove, Mills, Karter et al. (2020)** — the specific critique of the 2018 Lancet Commission. Use this to engage the establishment Lancet position critically without dismissing it.

**Position around (the empirical bridge):**

- **Kohrt et al. (2015)** *ENACT scale* — therapist competence in global mental health.
- **Singla, Kohrt, Murray et al. (2017)** — psychological treatments rest on relational *common factors*, not specific techniques.
- **Tol et al. (2011 *Lancet*; 2020 *Lancet GH* on SH+; 2023 *Lancet GH*)** — the sympathetic, methodologically rigorous bridge figure.
- **Purgato et al. (2018)** — IPD meta-analysis on focused psychosocial interventions for children: moderate effects on functioning, hope, coping, social support; **no** significant effect on depression/anxiety — important caveat that protects amna against narrow symptom-based comparisons.
- **Fazel, Reed, Panter-Brick & Stein (2012)** *Lancet* — parental mental health and family cohesion are the strongest protective factors for refugee children.
- **Atif Rahman's Thinking Healthy Programme** — peer-delivered intervention halved perinatal depression. Bridge to amna's lay/peer delivery model.
- **Theresa Betancourt** (LSWAY cohort; Family Strengthening Intervention) and **Catherine Panter-Brick** (biosocial refugee resilience).
- **Mark Jordans** (classroom-based psychosocial work in conflict settings).
- **Paul Farmer** *Accompaniment as Policy* (2011) — the conceptual home of amna's accompaniment model.

### 5.2 The field-positioning argument

**Not** "Amna is alternative" or "Amna is complementary." **Yes:** Amna is the decade-long operational implementation of where the empirical evidence and the decolonial critique are *both* converging.

The synthesis names **two evidence gaps amna sits inside and could close**:

1. **Accompaniment has never been isolated as an active ingredient in a humanitarian MHPSS trial** — despite being theorised by Farmer and operationalised at PIH and in lay-delivered models worldwide. Amna can credibly claim this gap.
2. **Communities of Practice as a *healing* mechanism for displaced populations themselves (not just as workforce development) is unevidenced.** Amna's CoP work is uniquely positioned to evidence this.

These become priority lines in the Future Research and Learning Agenda deliverable.

## 7. Premortem and safeguards

The act of naming risks at inception is itself the credibility move. Tabulate this in the inception note.

| # | Failure mode (imagined post-mortem) | Inception-stage safeguard (pre-commitment) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Overclaim.** Synthesis reads as causal proof of amna's model; readers infer effectiveness from coherence. | Pre-commit to language conventions: "evidence consistent with…", "CMO conjecture supported by…", *not* "demonstrates that…". Distinguish "claims with strong cross-pillar evidence" from "plausible but under-evidenced." Evidence & Claims Map operationalises this with the "Safe claim language" column. |
| 2 | **Ideological capture.** Synthesis reproduces amna's preferred narrative; refines the IPT but never *disconfirms* it. | Build in a **rival theory test**: each CMO conjecture is paired with a named alternative explanation that the evidence must be tested against. Document where rival theories better fit the data. |
| 3 | **Narrative flattening.** 11 pillars / 7+ contexts collapsed into a single tidy story that erases displacement-specific texture (Greek vs Afghan vs Ukrainian contexts have different operative dynamics). | Pre-commit to **context-stratified reporting**: every cross-cutting finding is accompanied by at least one "where this doesn't hold / looks different" example. Reserve space in the report structure for it. |
| 4 | **Methodological reductionism.** Lived-experience and practitioner-tacit evidence downgraded against published material. | Adopt a published appraisal logic (relevance + rigour) that treats grey, practitioner, and lived-experience sources as first-class evidence for the mechanisms they speak to. State this in the inception note's appraisal section with citations (Kothari et al.; ALNAP; practitioner-engaged synthesis literature). |
| 5 | **Partnership overload / extractive drift.** Amna staff over-burdened by sense-making asks, or consulted but not heard. | Cap partner-side time commitment (≤ X hours across project — to negotiate with Hejer at kick-off), pre-schedule the four touchpoints, name an amna-side single point of contact, and commit to a written "you said / we did" log after each sense-making session. |
