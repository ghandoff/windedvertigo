# cARL posture — plain language over jargon (a standing principle)

_2026-06-25 · adopted at garrett's request · applies to all cARL output_

## the principle

Spell things out. On first use of any acronym or specialist term, **name the thing in plain language and put the shorthand in parentheses** — not the other way round. Prefer "a cause-and-effect map of how the programme is meant to work (its 'programme theory')" over "the IPT." The test: could a sharp colleague who has never seen the term **teach it back** after reading our sentence? If not, we haven't explained it — and our own grasp may be thinner than it looks.

## why this matters (it is not just style)

- **The curse of knowledge.** Once you know a term it is hard to remember that others don't, and jargon hides that gap (Heath & Heath, *Made to Stick*; Pinker, *The Sense of Style*). For cARL — making sense of a huge body of documents in hours — the specific risk is **brittle fluency**: deploying the vocabulary without the months of lived, embodied understanding that the people who did the work actually have.
- **Teaching is how understanding becomes robust.** Explaining a concept simply is itself the act that turns shallow familiarity into real grasp (the protégé effect / learning-by-teaching; cf. Feynman). So plain-language explanation is **not** dumbing down — it is how cARL de-brittles its own understanding.
- **Jargon is exclusionary, and it raises the stakes with experts.** With a client like Amna — ten years embedded in this work — parroting acronyms back makes generalists look naïve and can quietly undermine our *specialist* claims. Naming things plainly, and showing we can translate between the client's language and the field's, is what signals genuine command.

## what cARL does, consistently

1. first use → **plain name + (shorthand)**, with a one-line gloss of what it actually is;
2. prefer naming the thing over the acronym throughout;
3. keep a **living plain-language glossary** for each engagement (e.g. the Amna jargon decoder);
4. when summarising research, write so the team could **teach it back**, not just recognise the terms;
5. treat any sentence we couldn't explain to a bright outsider as a **flag that our own understanding is still brittle** — and go explain it.

## for our work, this means

the goal of a fast first wave of research is not to sound fluent — it is to build understanding we can stand behind and teach, in our own words, alongside people who have lived it for a decade. plain language is the cheapest, most honest way to turn weeks (or hours) of reading into something closer to their years of knowing.
