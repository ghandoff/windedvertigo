# keeping a distributed, part-volunteer team moving between whirlpools

_cARL research brief · 2026-06-23 · requested by garrett_
_evidence-based playbook for async accountability + intrinsic motivation across timezones_

---

## the one-line diagnosis

our accountability is **synchronous-coupled** — it only fires when everyone is in the same room (mon/wed whirlpools). work drifts between sessions because the *structures* that produce follow-through (commitment, feedback, visibility) currently only exist live. the entire evidence base points one way: move accountability and motivation into the **written, async, visible layer** so it survives the gap between sessions — without tipping into surveillance, which would specifically poison our unpaid, passion-driven contributors.

a crucial constraint runs through everything below: **the levers that work for paid employees can actively backfire on volunteers.** two independent literatures (self-determination theory's over-justification effect, and volunteer crowding-out research) converge on the same warning — reaching for pay, stipends, or transactional "if-you-finish-x-then-y" deals to fix drift can *reduce* effort from jamie and lamis, not increase it.

---

## the evidence, and what each part means for us

### 1. self-determination theory — the engine for unpaid people

intrinsic motivation runs on three needs: **autonomy** (volition over what/how/when), **competence** (feeling effective and growing), and **relatedness** (belonging). support all three and self-motivation flourishes; thwart them and it collapses (ryan & deci, 2000, _american psychologist_ 55(1); ryan & deci, 2017).

the single most important result for our no-pay contributors is the **over-justification effect**: a meta-analysis of 128 experiments found that expected, tangible, contingent rewards significantly undermined intrinsic motivation — but **verbal praise and unexpected recognition *enhanced* it** (deci, koestner & ryan, 1999, _psychological bulletin_ 125(6)). the mechanism: every reward, deadline, or check-in carries a *controlling* aspect (pressures → undermines) and an *informational* aspect (affirms competence → builds). the same act does either, depending on how it's perceived.

_evidence quality:_ among the most validated frameworks in psychology, though a real counter-camp exists (cameron & pierce, 1994). the camps converge once moderators are isolated: expected/tangible/controlling rewards on already-interesting work are the risk; praise and recognition are safe.

**for our work, this means:** make autonomy, competence, and relatedness the design test for every ritual we add — before introducing any accountability mechanism, ask *does this support or control?* if it reads as control, it costs motivation, most expensively with jamie and lamis. for them, lean on **competence** (specific, informational feedback — "this reframing is exactly the threshold-concept move we needed," not "good job") and **relatedness** (visible belonging to the mission). do not introduce stipends or contingent perks to fix drift.

### 2. autonomy, mastery, purpose (pink) — the shared vocabulary

pink packages the academic work above into three plain-language drivers and argues "if-then" rewards reliably help only routine/algorithmic work; for creative/heuristic work they narrow focus and erode drive (pink, 2009, _drive_). a trade book, not primary research — use it as the team's shared language, anchor decisions in sdt. our work is heuristic and creative, exactly where carrots are weakest and purpose is strongest. our biggest asset: **the mission *is* the purpose** — name *why* a piece of work matters to learners every time, which disproportionately fuels jamie and lamis.

### 3. goal-setting theory — the fix for between-session drift

specific, challenging goals beat "do your best," because they direct attention, effort, and persistence (locke & latham, 2002, _american psychologist_ 57(9)). but the effect is **conditional** on four moderators: goal commitment, feedback on progress, adequate ability, and manageable complexity. commitment rises with participation and with **public/social commitment** (klein et al., 1999, meta-analysis of 83 samples). this is one of the most replicated findings in organisational psychology (~1,000 studies).

note our likely failure mode precisely: goals set on wednesday with **no feedback loop until monday** violate the feedback moderator — so the goals quietly die mid-week.

**counter-evidence — goals gone wild:** aggressive, narrow, single-metric goals backfire — tunnel vision, corner-cutting, reduced learning and intrinsic motivation (ordóñez et al., 2009). for novel/creative work where skill is still building, **learning goals** ("find and master three good methods") beat **performance goals** ("hit this number"), which drive anxiety (seijts & latham, 2005). a small mission-driven collective is exactly where performance-goal pressure does damage.

**for our work, this means:** end each whirlpool with each person stating **one specific, written, learning-framed commitment for before the next session** — "by monday i'll have drafted the x module intro," never a hard metric. this converts our reliable synchronous moment into a between-session goal *with public commitment* — the cheapest, highest-leverage change available.

### 4. psychological safety — the precondition, paired with accountability

psychological safety ("a shared belief the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking") predicts learning behaviour — asking for help, admitting you're behind, surfacing problems — which drives performance (edmondson, 1999; _the fearless organization_, 2018). google's project aristotle found it the #1 differentiator across 180 teams. critically, **safety is not lowered standards**: edmondson's 2×2 shows the high-performance "learning zone" needs **both** high safety *and* high accountability.

**for our work, this means:** the behaviour we most need between sessions is people *saying when they're stuck or behind* — that only happens if it's safe. make "i didn't get to x, here's what i need" an explicitly welcomed async move, and model it yourself, garrett. frame the whole accountability system as the both/and learning zone — high care **and** high standards — never as monitoring.

### 5. volunteer motivation — why jamie & lamis need different handling

volunteering serves six functions (the volunteer functions inventory): values, understanding, social, career, protective, enhancement (clary et al., 1998, _jpsp_ 74(6)). satisfaction and intention-to-continue rise when **the role is matched to the person's active motives** (stukas et al., 2009). retention is driven by recognition, development, and peer relationships — not money (forner et al., 2024, meta-analysis).

**the crowding-out warning:** introducing monetary payment to volunteers *reduced* their effort; it was the *fact* of payment, not its size, that undermined motivation (frey & goette, 1999), corroborated recently (dallmeyer et al., 2024). over-formalising also shrinks the motive-matching benefit.

**for our work, this means:** treat jamie and lamis as **volunteers, not cheap employees**. a short conversation to learn *which* motives drive each of them (likely values + understanding + enhancement for an education mission) lets us **match tasks to motives** — matched work gets done because they want to do it. this is our single best lever for their follow-through. recognition and development are the retention currency. avoid both traps: no stipends-to-fix-drift, and no over-bureaucratising their work.

### 6. async-first, written-first, visible work — the operating model

the practitioner canon for distributed work: **default to async, single source of truth, judge output not hours.** move communication from low-context (verbal/ephemeral) to high-context (written/durable); no expectation of instant replies (gitlab handbook; mullenweg's "five levels of distributed work," 2020; doist's "art of async"). **working out loud** = making work visible in shared spaces so a team's activity is legible *without* synchronous status updates (stepper, 2014). _these are descriptive handbooks, not controlled trials — but they are the field's reference models._

**for our work, this means:** decisions, commitments, and progress live in **one durable place** (our kanban/notion), not in chat or in people's heads. make work **visible by default** so follow-through is observable without anyone being chased — this is the structural substitute for synchronous-only accountability. set an explicit response-window norm (e.g. 24–48h) so "responsive" is defined. for payton specifically, a written board with clearly owned items removes prompt-dependence — the board does the prompting.

### 7. timezone fairness — don't over-weight the live rituals

cross-timezone synchronous meetings cluster into early-morning/late-evening slots, and the burden falls **asymmetrically** on those farther from the centre and lower in hierarchy (tang et al., microsoft research, chi 2023, ~20M meetings). a one-hour increase in temporal distance cuts synchronous communication ~11%, and much real-time work shifts to off-hours ("working around the clock," _organization science_, 2023/24). this is the **strongest, peer-reviewed** evidence in this brief.

**for our work, this means:** keep the whirlpools (they work), but (a) audit whose local clock they fall on — if anyone's slot is a 6am or 10pm, the "reliable accountability" we see may be partly *compliance under strain*, and the drift may be people reclaiming reasonable hours; and (b) make sure **nothing actionable requires live presence** — everything decided live should also live in writing, so an unfavourable timezone never means reduced influence.

### 8. accountability without surveillance — the concrete mechanisms

- **implementation intentions ("if-then" plans):** pre-linking a cue to a response ("*when* i sit down tuesday morning, i'll draft the intro") lifts goal attainment — **d = 0.65** across 94 tests / 8,000+ people (gollwitzer, 1999; gollwitzer & sheeran, 2006). free, evidence-backed, fully self-directed.
- **public commitment** works best when observable to peers (klein et al., 1999; bryan, karlan & nelson, 2010).
- **accountability boundary conditions:** it improves effort mainly when you're accountable to a *legitimate audience, known in advance, before forming your commitment* (lerner & tetlock, 1999) — so commitments should be stated up-front, to respected peers.
- **visualise work + limit WIP (kanban):** a shared board surfaces blockers for the whole team — transparency-based coordination, not individual monitoring (anderson, 2010). (this is exactly our "pam · team momentum" finding on WIP limits.)
- **the surveillance warning:** intensive electronic monitoring lowers trust and erodes intrinsic motivation (ravid et al., 2021). this is the precise mechanism that would backfire on jamie and lamis — never adopt activity/screen tracking.

## the playbook — what to actually do

A small, non-surveillant system that turns the reliable whirlpool into durable between-session accountability:

1. **close every whirlpool with written commitments.** each person states one specific, learning-framed commitment for before the next session, in writing, to the group. (goal-setting + public commitment + legitimate-audience-known-in-advance.)
2. **phrase them as if-then plans.** "when i [cue], i'll [action]" beats "i'll get to it." free, d=0.65, autonomy-preserving.
3. **one shared visible board as the single source of truth,** with WIP limits and clearly owned lanes. accountability becomes ambient and peer-visible, not top-down. everyone sees the same board; the work polices itself.
4. **one lightweight async midpoint check-in** — a written thread, not a meeting. satisfies the goal-setting "feedback" moderator and builds safety. ("what's done / what's stuck / what i need.")
5. **choose peer + visible accountability over monitoring, explicitly.** no activity-tracking tools — they would destroy the volunteer motivation that is our actual fuel.
6. **recognition is informational and unexpected,** never transactional. specific, mission-linked acknowledgement of competence.
7. **garrett's role shifts** from chaser to *designer of the visible system + giver of informational feedback*. that's how you get between-session accountability without micromanaging.

### tailored to each person

- **jamie & lamis (passion-driven volunteers):** match tasks to their active motives; keep accountability invitational and visible, never transactional; recognition + development as currency; no pay-based fixes. the board lets them see their contribution to the mission (relatedness + competence).
- **payton (reactive):** reactivity is usually a clarity gap, not a willingness gap. give an **owned board lane** + **if-then plans** so initiating has an obvious home and the board does the prompting. safety to say "i'm not sure what to start on" turns silence into a question you can answer.
- **maria (high performer):** protect autonomy, feed mastery with stretch scope; let her **model the public-commitment norm**. watch she isn't silently absorbing others' drift (a fairness/relatedness risk).

## tensions to keep visible

1. accountability structures can themselves dent intrinsic motivation if they read as controlling — design every one to be self-chosen, peer-visible, and feedback-rich.
2. goal-setting backfires on creative work as hard single metrics — use learning/process goals.
3. money is a hazard, not a lever, for jamie & lamis — recognition and matched-meaningful-work instead.
4. synchronous rituals are biased against unfavourable timezones — keep the whirlpools but make nothing depend on live presence.
5. the strongest evidence here is mixed in provenance: the psychology (sdt, goal-setting, implementation intentions, safety, VFI) is peer-reviewed and robust; the async operating model (gitlab/automattic/doist) is practitioner canon, not tested. the recommendations hold because the themes *converge*, not because any single source is definitive.
