01 — orientation

you have good instincts.
let's make them rigorous.

history co:lab is doing something genuinely rare: building skill progressions for civics learning that are grounded in what students actually need to do — not just know. That instinct is right. But the next challenge is one that stumps most educators, even well-resourced ones: how do you actually see those skills developing?

The gap we keep encountering: skill frameworks describe competencies beautifully — critical thinking, civic agency, perspective-taking — but when it comes to measuring growth, most programs fall back on pre/post surveys, reflection journals, or teacher ratings. These matter. But they leave a lot of the richest signal on the floor.

This document is a primer — not a textbook — on the full toolkit for observing, capturing, and making sense of how skills develop. It draws on our direct work across four continents and on the published research of the team we're proposing to bring to this collaboration. By the end, you'll understand both the state of the art and the specific approach we'd propose for your civics skill progressions.

02 — evidence spectrum

not all evidence is equal.
drag to explore.

Evidence exists on a spectrum — from self-reported opinion to in-situ behavioral observation. Each level has different costs, different validity, and different uses. The goal isn't always to get to the deepest level. It's to know which level you need for the claims you want to make.

drag the slider to explore each level of evidence
self-report structured tasks behavioral stealth / in-situ
level 1 of 4 — lowest inference required

self-report

Surveys, reflections, and questionnaires where learners describe their own beliefs, experiences, or skill levels. Fast, inexpensive, and useful for capturing perceived growth — but highly susceptible to social desirability bias and disconnect between stated and actual behavior.

civics example: "On a scale of 1–5, how confident are you in your ability to evaluate a news source?" — captures perception, not performance.
03 — the toolkit

evidence gathering lives in

The frontier of learning science has dramatically expanded the methods available for capturing skill development. These methods span physical classrooms, digital environments, and hybrid experiences — each with different affordances for civic learning contexts.

🎥
physical

video coding

Structured analysis of classroom recordings using behavioral coding guides. Captures non-verbal, interactional, and procedural indicators that surveys miss entirely.

🎙️
physical

audio capture & discourse analysis

Recording and coding student dialogue during discussions, simulations, and deliberation tasks — one of the richest signal sources for civic reasoning skills.

📡
physical

RFID & spatial tracking

Radio-frequency tags or low-cost sensors that capture movement patterns, collaboration networks, and spatial choices in physical environments.

📝
physical

artifact analysis

Systematic rubric-based scoring of student-created products — documents, maps, arguments, designs — as proxies for underlying skill development.

📊
digital

log file & clickstream data

Behavioral telemetry captured automatically as students interact with digital tools. Includes time-on-task, navigation patterns, revision behavior, help-seeking, and persistence.

🎭
digital

Structured scenarios that present realistic dilemmas requiring skill-aligned choices. A psychometrically rigorous alternative to surveys that measures behavioral intent rather than self-perception.

🕵️

Invisible, continuous measurement embedded within learning activities — learners never "take a test." Evidence is inferred from performance traces in real-time digital environments.

🎮

🎥 video coding — deeper dive

Video coding requires two things: a well-designed behavioral coding guide (linked to your skill framework) and trained coders. The power comes from the richness of what can be observed — not just what students say, but how they say it, who they look at, whether they defer or challenge.

  • Micro-level indicators: turn-taking, interruptions, eye contact patterns, gesture use, facial expressions during deliberation
  • Macro-level indicators: group decision-making processes, equity of participation, argumentation quality
  • Tools: BORIS, ELAN, Mangold Interact, Noldus Observer XT, or low-cost setups using DOTE
  • Inter-rater reliability: requires at least two independent coders — Cohen's κ ≥ 0.70 is the minimum bar for publishable claims
Funder signal: NSF, Spencer Foundation, and MacArthur all flag "behavioral evidence" as a differentiator in civic learning proposals. Video-coded data paired with a published coding guide can triple the credibility of outcome claims.

🎙️ audio capture & discourse analysis

Structured discussion is arguably the most important site for civic skill development — and audio is its primary trace. Discourse analysis can reveal reasoning quality, perspective-taking, evidence use, and civic knowledge application in ways no survey can touch.

  • Automated: AI transcription (Otter.ai, Whisper) + computational analysis of argument structure, vocabulary sophistication, epistemic markers
  • Human-coded: Accountable Talk coding, Toulmin argumentation coding, civic reasoning coding schemes
  • Emerging: prosodic analysis (tone, pace, confidence markers) as soft indicators of civic agency
  • Low-lift implementation: 5-minute deliberation clips analyzed post-session
Funder signal: CivXNow coalition and iCivics funders have signaled strong interest in discourse-level evidence of democratic reasoning. This is an underserved measurement niche.

📡 RFID & spatial tracking

RFID tags — inexpensive passive chips worn as badges — can map which students interact with which, how often, and in what spaces. Originally used in epidemiology research, they've been adapted by learning researchers to study collaboration networks and equity of participation in physical classrooms.

  • Passive RFID: detects proximity (within ~1.5m) — cheap, no GPS, privacy-preserving
  • Sociometric badges (Sociometric Solutions): capture proximity + voice activity + body movement simultaneously
  • Use case for civics: mapping who collaborates with whom in role-play simulations, tracking whether marginalized voices engage in deliberation tasks
  • Regulatory note: FERPA and COPPA compliance is straightforward — data is proximity-only, not location
Funder signal: NSF STEM+C has funded RFID-based learning studies. The novelty of the method is genuinely compelling to reviewers — few civic learning proposals have used it.

📝 artifact analysis

Student-created documents, arguments, civic proposals, and projects can be analyzed systematically using rubrics anchored to your skill progressions. The key is the quality of the rubric — and whether it produces reliable scores across raters.

  • What to score: evidence selection, argument construction, acknowledgment of multiple perspectives, action orientation, revision quality
  • Rubric design: 4-level behavioral rubrics perform better than holistic rubrics for inter-rater reliability and for providing actionable feedback
  • w.v tool: the rubric co-builder lets educators and students co-construct rubrics live in a classroom setting
Funder signal: Portfolio-based evidence is central to Carnegie Corporation's civic education priorities. Paired with a validity argument, it's compelling.

📊 log file & clickstream data

Every digital tool generates behavioral trace data as a byproduct of normal use. When mapped to a skill framework, these traces become psychometrically meaningful — without requiring students to do anything beyond their normal learning activity.

  • What it captures: dwell time on evidence sources, sequence of resource access, revision frequency, help-seeking behavior, time distribution across task phases
  • Analytic approaches: process mining, sequence analysis, IRT-based latent trait estimation from process variables
  • OECD PISA 2021: creative thinking assessment used log file data to score inquiry process quality alongside final products
  • Privacy: log data is behavioral, not content — it shows how students work, not what they wrote
Funder signal: Any funder interested in learning analytics or AI-in-education will find log-based evidence compelling. It demonstrates technical capacity and scalable measurement.

🎭 situational judgment tasks (SJTs)

SJTs present realistic dilemmas and ask respondents to evaluate possible responses — rather than describe their own behavior. Decades of organizational psychology research shows they outperform self-report surveys for predicting actual behavior. They are particularly powerful for social and civic skills because they surface what people would do, not what they think they should say.

  • Structure: scenario stem + 4–6 response options → scored for skill-aligned reasoning quality
  • Scoring models: consensus scoring (most effective), expert-rated ideal, or empirical keying
  • Civic applications: deliberation scenarios, source evaluation dilemmas, civic action choice tasks
  • Co-creation: scenarios generated with students produce higher ecological validity and become learning activities themselves
Funder signal: SJTs are published in peer-reviewed journals and recognized by the American Educational Research Association as a valid measurement approach. Referencing them in a proposal signals methodological sophistication.

🕵️ stealth assessment — the frontier

Stealth assessment — a term coined by Valerie Shute at Florida State — embeds measurement invisibly within learning activities so that the act of learning is the act of assessment. Garrett Jaeger led the application of this approach at the LEGO Foundation across programmatic activities in multiple countries, mapping exploratory behaviors to creativity and play-based skill development.

  • Core mechanism: evidence-centered design (ECD) — defines what skills look like in observable behavior before designing the activity
  • In physical spaces: video-coded behavioral traces, spatial choice patterns, object manipulation sequences
  • In digital spaces: interaction telemetry, process variables, response time distributions
  • Key insight (from Garrett's LEGO work): the shift is not from bad measurement to good — it's from measuring knowledge of to measuring engagement in
Funder signal: NSF, Spencer Foundation, and the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation have all funded stealth assessment research. The term alone in a proposal signals methodological currency.

🎮 gamified performance tasks

Game-based assessment generates rich behavioral evidence under high motivation and authentic decision-making conditions. Unlike traditional tests, performance tasks in designed play experiences capture not just final answers but the process of arriving at them — a much richer signal for skill progressions.

  • Research base: Kristin Lansing's Circuit Runner study (500 participants, Amazon Mechanical Turk) used a first-person game with a virtual agent to generate telemetry-based collaborative problem-solving evidence
  • Civic learning application: city council simulation games, historical role-play with consequence tracking, deliberation tools with embedded behavioral logging
  • Key design principle: separate the behavioral signal from the gameplay reward structure to avoid confounding evidence
Funder signal: Digital Promise, the Joan Ganz Cooney Center, and MacArthur's Connected Learning have specifically funded game-based civic learning work.

🤝 co-created SJT scenarios — winded.vertigo's method

One of the most powerful innovations we've developed is using scenario co-creation as both a learning activity and a source of authentic assessment material. Educators and students generate the scenarios — drawing on real or realistic experiences — and in the process reveal their own mental models of what the skill looks like in practice.

  • Process: participants describe a "turbulence moment" — a real dilemma they've faced — and then list possible responses and the skills each demonstrates
  • What it generates: a library of authentic scenarios that become SJT items, co-created by the very community they'll assess
  • Civics version: "describe a moment when you had to decide whether to act on a civic issue — what made it hard, what did you consider, what did you do?"
  • Dual purpose: the act of scenario generation is itself a civic reasoning task, observable and codeable
Funder signal: Participatory research approaches are explicitly valued by the Spencer Foundation and W.K. Kellogg Foundation. Co-creation is a methodological and values statement simultaneously.

🔮 augmented reality contexts

AR creates structured observation contexts in physical spaces — overlaying prompts, information, and interaction triggers on the physical environment. winded.vertigo has deployed AR within the PRME Pedagogy Certificate System in collaboration with the UN Global Compact, creating behavioral contexts for evidence gathering in business school classrooms globally.

  • Evidence types: decision-making in augmented contexts, response to scenario triggers, peer interaction patterns
  • Classroom implementation: AR can be deployed on smartphones — no specialized hardware required
  • Civics application: local government simulations, historical site overlays, civic issue mapping tasks
Funder signal: AR-enabled assessment is a notable differentiator — few civic education programs use it, and several NSF and IMLS programs have flagged it as an area of interest.

🧩 rubric co-builder — live tool

winded.vertigo's rubric co-builder (live at windedvertigo.com) enables real-time collaborative rubric construction in classroom settings. Educators and students work together to articulate what each level of a skill looks like in observable behavior — producing both a valid measurement instrument and a powerful learning activity simultaneously.

  • Live tool: windedvertigo.com/harbour/rubric-co-builder
Funder signal: tools that generate evidence and build educator capacity in the same activity are highly fundable — they solve two problems at once.

📁 portfolio + digital trace integration

Portfolios are powerful because they capture student-curated evidence of growth over time. But they become dramatically more rigorous when combined with digital trace data from the platforms students use to create them — linking product quality (what you made) to process data (how you made it).

  • Example: a student's civic action proposal (artifact) paired with document revision history, research trail, and collaboration log (trace data)
  • Technical implementation: Google Workspace, Notion, or purpose-built platforms all expose log data via API
  • What it enables: distinguishing students who produced high-quality work through high-quality process from those who produced it through other means
Funder signal: Portfolio-based evidence is central to many civic education funders' theories of change. Pairing it with process data addresses the validity critique they often raise.

interactive

civics SJT — scenario player

scenario 1 of 3 — source evaluation
Your class is preparing a civic action proposal on water quality in your city. You find a detailed report claiming local tap water is unsafe. The report is published on a professional-looking website. You notice the site has no "About" page, no listed author, and was registered three months ago. It cites a study you can't find anywhere else. Your group wants to use it as your lead source.
You would most likely:
A Point out the missing author and registration date to your group, then suggest you look for the original study or a corroborating source before deciding whether to use it.
B Use the report — it supports your position and your proposal needs strong evidence. You can note the limitations in a footnote.
C Suggest dropping the report entirely — you don't want to risk your credibility on a questionable source.
D Ask the most tech-savvy person in your group to check if the website is legitimate and go with their verdict.

scenario 2 of 3 — civic action
Your class has been studying housing policy in your city. Several students — including you — want to attend a city council meeting to speak during public comment. But one member of your group says the council "won't listen anyway" and that going is a waste of time. Another says she wants to make a social media video instead, which would reach more people.
You would most likely:
A Acknowledge both concerns, then suggest both actions — the city council comment creates an official record and demonstrates formal participation, while the social media video builds community awareness. Different tools for different purposes.
B Agree that the council probably won't listen and support the social media idea instead — it has more reach.
C Argue strongly for the city council meeting — public comment is a formal right, and dismissing it is giving up too quickly.
D Stay quiet and let the group decide — you don't want to create conflict over which approach to take.

scenario 3 of 3 — perspective-taking
Your class is doing a simulation of a zoning decision. You've been assigned to represent a neighborhood business coalition that opposes a new affordable housing development. Personally, you strongly support affordable housing. The simulation asks you to make the coalition's strongest possible case.
You would most likely:
A Research the business coalition's actual concerns, find their strongest arguments (economic impact, parking, community character), and make their case as compellingly as you can — even though you disagree.
B Tell the class you personally support affordable housing before making the coalition's case — it doesn't feel right to argue something you don't believe without disclosure.
C Make the case while subtly steering toward arguments that could actually support affordable housing — you can't argue for something you think is wrong.
D Ask to be reassigned to a different role — you shouldn't have to argue for a position you strongly disagree with.

what a fundable evidence argument looks like

1

evidence-centered design (ECD)

Define, collaboratively: what are the skills? What would it look like if a student had them? What could we observe? This is the foundational step — and the rubric co-builder is how we facilitate it with your educators.

2

co-create the measurement instruments

Develop SJTs, behavioral coding guides, and rubrics alongside your educators and students. The co-creation process is itself publishable as a methodology — and it produces instruments with higher ecological validity.

3

deploy a multi-modal evidence strategy

Select 2–3 evidence types matched to your implementation context — no need to do everything. A well-designed SJT + audio-coded deliberation + artifact rubric is already a strong package for most funders.

4

pre-register the study design

winded.vertigo registers studies on the Open Science Framework (OSF) — a practice that dramatically increases proposal credibility with funders like NSF, Spencer, and Carnegie.

5

publish the framework + findings

A published behavioral indicator framework for civics skill progressions would be a genuine contribution to the field — and one that supports future funding cycles. Garrett has authored in this area and Kristin has worked on OECD-scale assessment frameworks.

08 — what comes next

what a collaboration looks like

We don't pitch measurement systems — we design them with you. Here's a sketch of what early partnership could look like, based on where history co:lab is.

phase 0 — discovery

A structured conversation about your skill progressions, your classroom contexts, and what observable behaviors you've already noticed but don't currently measure. We listen first.

phase 1 — rubric sprint

A facilitated session using the rubric co-builder to produce behavioral indicators for 2–3 of your core civics skills. Educators and students participate. The output is immediately usable.

phase 2 — sjt design

Co-create a set of SJT scenarios grounded in real civics dilemmas your students face. Pilot with one cohort. Score and interpret the results together.

phase 3 — standards alignment

Map your skills progressions to national and local standards frameworks, enabling the same instruments to serve both formative (classroom) and summative (program/funder) purposes without double-work.

phase 4 — proposal build

With a working evidence design in hand, we help you construct the measurement section of a funding proposal — the part most organizations get wrong. We've done this for Spencer, Carnegie, and NSF-aligned funders. Further down the road: depth.chart for cohort tracking, AI-scored deliberation analysis, and custom game-based assessment.
