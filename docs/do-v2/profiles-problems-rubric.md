# windedvertigo.com v-next — profiles & problems rubric

*Prepared from the Jul 22 FruitStand meeting (Gemini + Notion transcripts) and the Marketing Strategy doc, §1.3. This is the "customer type + problems" rubric Fruit asked for at the end of the meeting.*

---

## Part 1 — The core visitor profiles (distilled to five)

The meeting's key structural insight (Fruit): **profiles don't map 1:1 to families** — "we have seven families, but there's really just like four types of people coming," and "if you try and market to everyone, you're marketing to no one." So the rubric works on two axes: a small set of cross-cutting profiles, each of which enters through different families. These five distill the brainstormed list (orgs wanting to show impact, researchers, faculty, parents, teachers, NGOs, corporate, data scientists, preschool teachers, professors) plus the port's existing segments (K-12 educators, higher-ed faculty, learning designers, institutions, parents/families, edtech):

**P1 · The Accountable Program Leader** — program directors, MEL leads, and founders at foundations, INGOs, NGOs, and ministries. They owe a funder or board an answer about whether their work *works*. Budget authority, allergic to two-year evaluations and overclaiming. *Primary families: 1, 7, 6. Secondary: 2, 4.* (Amna archetype.)

**P2 · The Research & Insights Director** — R&D, insights, and data leads at consumer-goods, edtech, and assessment companies — "could be f***ing any field." Has a data problem and a budget, but no ML team. Includes the data-scientist wink audience drowning in cleaning and coding. *Primary families: 2, 1.* (Nordic archetype.)

**P3 · The Educator-Developer** — higher-ed faculty, PD coordinators, university networks, UN-aligned bodies (PRME), and school/CPD leaders who need credible, credentialed learning that actually reaches classrooms — often across countries and languages. *Primary families: 3, 6, 5.* (PPCS archetype.)

**P4 · The Community Convener** — association and foundation event owners, network and consortium leads who bring a field together and are tired of dead Zoom walls and slide-fests. *Primary families: 4, 7, 5.* (ATP/press.play archetype.)

**P5 · The Curious Player** — parents, teachers, and kids who arrive through the games; plus curious professionals who want to play with our thinking before ever buying an engagement. Not a revenue profile — a top-of-funnel and trust profile that feeds P1–P4. *Primary families: 5, then everywhere.* (Harbour archetype.)

Per the meeting's "Pixar factor": every family page should speak plainly to its primary profile and carry a wink only its sector would catch — "only you would know what we're talking about here."

## Part 3 — Corrections to the current table

- **Row 2, profiles:** "catsup to the ChatGPT world" → "catch up to the ChatGPT world" (transcription typo — unless we're keeping it as the first wink).
- **Row 1, problems:** the current three lines ("Having difficult showing impact / Don't know how to evidence what we do / Don't know how to translate evidence into practice or policy") are right in substance but written from *our* side, not the visitor's. Rewritten above in the problem-first voice the meeting aligned on, and expanded with the flexibility/speed problem Garrett named via Amna ("five years ago it was impossible to be flexible with measurement… now we can do it instantaneously").
- **Row 2, problems:** "How can our humans spend less time doing data entry" kept, but broadened — data entry is one symptom; the family also solves "don't know where to start," "knowledge trapped in PDFs," and "window of time" urgency.
- **Row 3, profiles:** "faculty" was too narrow — the actual buyer (per §2 beachheads) is the PD coordinator / PRME-type body; faculty are participants. Both now listed.
- **Row 5, profiles:** kept Parents/Teachers, added the profile the July memo says is the real commercial buyer: programs wanting productized MEL. Harbour's "buyer is attention" — it feeds Families 1 and 3.
- **Rows 4, 6, 7:** were empty; filled from beachhead analysis (§2) + transcript discussion.

## Part 4 — How this feeds the site mechanics (for Friday)

The meeting's decision-tree/slider concept needs exactly this rubric as its content: the entry question isn't "what service do you want?" but a profile self-select ("that's me") followed by the problem hook ("yeah, I'm bloated all the time"), then the family reveal, then portfolio proof ("here's how we've done it before, so you can trust us"). Five profiles × the problems above = the script for the choose-your-own-adventure branches, the short-form FOMO reels ("here's a problem, here's a solution, here's a FOMO"), and the SEO/AEO question inventory — these problem statements are, nearly verbatim, what someone would type into an LLM before they know we exist.
