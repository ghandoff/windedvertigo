## 1. creaseworks: the FIND stage — breakout play + co-design planning (30 min)

### the brief (5 min — garrett)

the FIND stage is where a child's playdate begins: they go hunting for materials around their house or school before any making starts. right now we have a limited set of FIND prompts, and we haven't yet involved children in designing them.

two goals this session:
1. **generate viable FIND prototypes** — variations that are polished enough to put in front of children for co-design feedback.
2. **sketch a co-design research plan** — how do we get kids involved in shaping the FIND stage before we lock it in?

context from research (cARL): the joan ganz cooney center (sesame workshop's research arm) runs the **sandbox** initiative, an applied R&D programme that helps digital product teams co-design with children. their partner, **the GIANT room** (NYC), facilitates in-person sessions where kids ages 3–12 playtest, give structured feedback, and prototype new ideas using everyday materials. critical finding: when children were asked to co-design collaborative games, **scavenger hunts with clues emerged organically** as a preferred mechanic — directly validating our FIND stage concept.

guiding questions:
- what makes a scavenger hunt exciting vs. tedious?
- how do we honour "use what you have" while still creating surprise?
- what does FIND look like for a 5-year-old vs. a 10-year-old?
- can FIND itself be a threshold experience — where the searching changes how the child sees their environment?
- **new**: which FIND variations are ready to show a child? which need another iteration first?

### breakout rooms (15 min — 2-3 per room)

each room plays a different harbour game or creaseworks prototype. while playing, note:
- what the FIND equivalent is (or could be) in that game
- one new FIND variation inspired by the game's mechanic
- whether the game falls into "a different colour of the same experience" (garrett's fruitstand concern) or genuinely varies the mechanic
- **could a child playtest this FIND variation today?** rate it: ready / almost / needs work

suggested room assignments:
- **room 1**: rhythm lab — what does "finding sounds" look like?
- **room 2**: lines become loops / tidal pool — finding shapes, patterns, textures
- **room 3**: values auction / joy box campfire — finding objects with stories or meaning attached

### reconvene + co-design planning (10 min)

each room shares: one FIND variation, one surprise, one question. then shift to planning:

**co-design research plan — starter proposal** (adapted from the cooney center sandbox / GIANT room model):

- **method**: playtest + prototype sessions. children play our FIND prototypes, give feedback, then invent their own FIND variations using physical materials (paper, markers, found objects).
- **who**: 8–12 children, ages 5–10, in 2–3 small groups (2 adults per group as listeners, not instructors). prioritise diverse backgrounds per w.v's participatory co-design commitment.
- **where**: virtual (via creaseworks) or in-person if a partner school/org can host. consider the GIANT room (NYC), a local library, or a nordic partner for the first session.
- **duration**: 60–90 minutes per session, 2–3 sessions over 3 weeks.
- **compensation**: $25–50 per family (align with cooney center norms).
- **what we learn**: which FIND mechanics children find genuinely fun vs. confusing, new variations we haven't imagined, age-specific differences (5 vs. 10), accessibility gaps.
- **roles**: druin's ladder — start children as **testers** (playtest existing FIND), then elevate to **design partners** (co-create new FIND variations alongside the team).
- **timeline**: pilot session by end of june / early july. first findings fed back into creaseworks before nordic documentation deadline (june 20).
- **tools to explore**: cooney center sandbox playtest toolkit (playtestwithkids.org), the GIANT room's facilitation model, sesame workshop's Kids Included report.

**decisions needed today**: who leads the co-design sprint? do we partner with an existing org (GIANT room, a school) or run it ourselves? virtual first or in-person?
